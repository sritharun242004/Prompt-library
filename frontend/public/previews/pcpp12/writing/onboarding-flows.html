The Hidden Complexity of Onboarding Flows

Onboarding is where most SaaS products lose half their trial users. The best flows share a non-obvious structural property that most teams never articulate.

OnboardingSaaSUX

Most SaaS onboarding flows are designed from the product's perspective, not the user's. They ask: what does the user need to configure to use our product? The better question is: what is the minimum the user needs to do to experience the product's core value? These two questions yield radically different flows.

The structural property shared by the best onboarding flows — the one that most teams never consciously identify — is progressive commitment. They do not ask for information they do not need immediately. They defer configuration to the moment it is relevant rather than front-loading a multi-step setup wizard. The user's first meaningful interaction with the product happens within 60 seconds of signing up, before they have invested anything emotionally.

The hardest engineering problem in onboarding is sample data. Every new user arrives to an empty state. If your product requires data to be useful, you have to manufacture that data convincingly. The options are: import from existing tools (highest friction, highest value), sample data (lowest friction, lowest personalisation), or guided creation (medium friction, high completion rate if the steps are atomic enough). Most products choose sample data and underinvest in making it feel personally relevant.

The most neglected phase of onboarding is re-onboarding: what happens when a user returns after 2 weeks of inactivity? Almost no products have a coherent answer. The opportunity is large — a well-designed re-engagement email plus a contextual "pick up where you left off" surface in the product can recover a significant fraction of churned trials. Design these states with the same rigour you give first-run onboarding.

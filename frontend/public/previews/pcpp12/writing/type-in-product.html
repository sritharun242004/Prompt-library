Typography as UX: The Decisions That Matter

Font choices get attention, but line height, measure, and optical alignment are what actually determine whether text is readable at scale.

TypographyDesign SystemsCSS

Every design team debates font choices. Serif or sans? System font or custom? Those debates are rarely the right ones to have. The typographic decisions that actually affect product quality are quieter and harder to see in a static mockup: line height, measure (line length), letter-spacing, and optical alignment.

Measure is the most commonly violated typographic rule in product design. The ideal range for body text is 60–75 characters per line. In practice, most product UIs run full-width containers at large viewport sizes, producing measures of 120+ characters. The result is an interface that looks fine at a glance but is exhausting to actually read. Adding a max-width to body text containers is one of the cheapest, highest-ROI design improvements you can make.

Line height deserves its own section. The default browser line-height of 1.2 is appropriate for headings only. Body text needs 1.5–1.75 to breathe. Small text (11–12px) needs 1.8 or higher, because the optical density of small type increases. These are not aesthetic preferences — they are legibility requirements that have been tested across millions of data points.

Optical alignment is the most advanced and most neglected. When you place a text label inside a badge or button, the visual centre of gravity of the text sits lower than the mathematical centre because of cap height and descenders. The fix is to add 1–2px of top padding relative to bottom padding. It is invisible when done right and jarring when not done.

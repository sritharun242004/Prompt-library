Himalayan Passes

The high routes between India and Tibet before the snows come

The Himalayan passes have always been more than landscape. They are the infrastructure of a civilisation — the routes through which Buddhism moved from India to Tibet, through which trade moved in both directions for two millennia, through which armies moved with results that shaped the modern borders of China, India, and Pakistan. They are also, currently, among the most beautiful and most fragile landscapes on earth, changing faster than our political arrangements can accommodate.

I made this series over three seasons, from 2019 to 2021, travelling the routes between Manali and Leh, through the Spiti Valley, and across the Zanskar range. What I was looking for was the human presence in a landscape that is typically photographed as if it were empty — as if the people who have lived here for centuries were merely incidental to the geology. They are not incidental. They are the reason any of this is comprehensible.

The Gaddi shepherds, the BRO workers, the monks, the mule-drivers: these are the people who make the passes legible. Without them, the landscape is spectacular but mute. With them, it becomes a story — of adaptation, of knowledge, of the particular kind of courage required to live at altitude in a climate that is attempting, with increasing urgency, to become unliveable.

The passes are closing earlier now and opening later. The glaciers that feed the streams and springs on which every village in this landscape depends are retreating at rates that local people can observe with the naked eye over a single lifetime. I am not a climate photographer; I resist the reduction of landscape to crisis. But the crisis is present in these images, and I would be dishonest to pretend otherwise.

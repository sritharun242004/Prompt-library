The Last Dhow

Traditional wooden shipbuilding at Beypore and Mandvi

A dhow is an argument against efficiency. It takes three years to build, requires no blueprints, employs forty men, and uses no metal fasteners in its hull. The resulting vessel will sail the Arabian Sea for a century. This is not romantic inefficiency — it is a different calculus entirely, one in which time is cheap and materials are expensive, and in which the knowledge required to build correctly is rare enough to command respect.

The Beypore shipyard on the Kerala coast is one of the last places in the world where the urus — the largest type of wooden dhow — is still built by hand. The craftsmen who work here are Khalasis and Muhamedans whose families have built boats for the Gulf trade for over 1,500 years. The ships they produce are commissioned almost entirely by buyers from Oman, Kuwait, and the UAE, who use them as heritage vessels, pleasure craft, and the occasional working cargo ship.

I spent six weeks at Beypore and a further two weeks at the smaller Mandvi yard in Gujarat, attempting to understand a craft that operates entirely on embodied knowledge. The master builder Syed Rasheed, who has constructed twenty-three dhows in his career, works from memory and from the inherited tradition he calls andaz — a word that means estimation but implies something closer to instinct refined by decades of practice. When I asked him how he knew whether a rib was the right curve, he held out his hand and described an arc with his thumb.

There is a paradox at the centre of this work: the vessels are built for clients who value the tradition, and the tradition is kept alive by the clients who commission it. Without the Gulf buyers, there would be no Beypore yard. Without the yard, there would be no tradition. I am not sure whether this is heritage preservation or something stranger — a craft maintained in amber by an economy of nostalgia.

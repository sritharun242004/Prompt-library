The Weavers of Banaras

Inside the khatri households where silk still speaks in thread

There is a proverb among the weavers of Varanasi: a loom is a prayer. I heard it in the narrow galis of the old city, spoken without irony by men who have spent fifty years at their handlooms and consider themselves neither artists nor craftsmen but practitioners of something older than both those categories. The Banarasi weaving tradition is at minimum five hundred years old, and it is, depending on who you ask, either flourishing or dying.

The truth is more complicated. The GI tag has brought international visibility to the finest katan silk and tissue sarees. There are buyers in Tokyo and Milan who know what a Banarasi brocade is. Meanwhile, in the same narrow lanes, power looms are producing machine-made versions of the same patterns for a fraction of the price, and the young men who would once have apprenticed at a handloom are driving auto-rickshaws instead. Both things are true simultaneously, and neither cancels the other.

I photographed the Ansari and Khatri families of the Madanpura and Alaipura districts over eight months, returning at different hours, in different seasons, attempting to understand not just the craft but the social ecology that sustains it. What I found was a community under pressure that had not yet broken — and that continued to produce, on handlooms in rooms barely ten feet across, some of the most complex fabric in the world.

The finest piece I witnessed being made was a double-woven katan silk in crimson and gold, commissioned by a family in Mumbai for a daughter's wedding. It took two brothers nineteen days working in shifts. When they finished, the elder brother held it to the light without speaking. Then he folded it and handed it over. That silence was worth all the photographs I made.

The Wrestlers

Inside the akharas of Varanasi, where the body is a temple

The akhara is a specific kind of space: a clay-pit gymnasium, temple, and moral institution simultaneously. The traditional Indian wrestling practice of kushti has been conducted in these spaces for at least four hundred years in Varanasi, under a discipline that is explicitly religious — the body is treated as a form of worship, the wrestler as a devotee of Hanuman — and that involves a regime of training, diet, and behaviour that would be considered extreme in any other context. The wrestlers do not drink alcohol, do not eat meat, do not stay up past ten o'clock at night, and spend four to five hours a day in physical training.

I spent two months at three Varanasi akharas in the winter of 2018, photographing the daily life of the institution. What I found was a form of discipline that was both genuinely rigorous and genuinely communal — the senior wrestlers taught the junior without payment, the akhara provided food and space for wrestlers who could not afford it, and the entire enterprise was sustained by a combination of private patronage, municipal support, and the sheer commitment of the people involved.

The tradition is under pressure. The physical demands of the sport, combined with the economic impossibility of sustaining the traditional diet on a wrestler's income, mean that most practitioners must find other employment. The akharas are full of men who wrestle at 5am and drive auto-rickshaws at 9am. The tradition persists because the people who practise it love it more than they can rationally account for. This is, I think, the condition of every traditional practice: it survives not because it is efficient but because it is chosen.

The photographs in this series are an attempt to document a practice that has, so far, survived the twentieth century and is now negotiating the twenty-first. The negotiation is still ongoing. I do not know how it will end. I suspect the akharas will still be there — smaller, perhaps, and more self-consciously traditional than they once were — when I come back in ten years.

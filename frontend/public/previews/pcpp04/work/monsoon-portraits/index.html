Monsoon Portraits

Faces from the first week of the Kerala rains

The Kerala monsoon arrives like a decree. In the first week of June, the southwest wind builds over the Arabian Sea and breaks against the Western Ghats with the force of an argument that has been building for six months. It does not rain — it roars. The rivers become the colour of red earth. The paddy fields become mirrors. The coconut palms lean sideways and come back upright.

I have photographed the monsoon three times, and each time I am unprepared for it. Not for the rain itself — I know rain — but for what it does to people. There is a particular quality of attention that Malayalis bring to the monsoon that I have not found anywhere else: a combination of resignation, pleasure, and the deeply practical attitude of people who have been living with this for 3,000 years. Nobody runs for cover. Nobody curses it. They simply walk more slowly and get wet.

These portraits were made in the first seven days of the 2023 monsoon in Thiruvananthapuram, Alappuzha, and Thrissur. I used no artificial light. I was soaked for a week. The camera survived; so did I, more or less. What I wanted was not the drama of the rain but the ordinariness of people living inside it — the faces that have stopped noticing.

What I found instead were faces full of noticing. Every person I photographed was watching the rain even when they appeared to be ignoring it. The elderly woman on the veranda, the card players under the tamarind, the nurse between patients — all of them were attending to the season with a consciousness that I, behind my camera, could only approximate.

Bengal Winter

The brief cold season that transforms everything

The Bengali relationship with winter is the relationship of a people who live in a hot climate with the one season that is not hot. The Bengalis have turned this eight-week reprieve into a minor art form. The winter brings the date palm jaggery — nolen gur — that flavours the sweets and desserts of the season. It brings the melas, the football tournaments, the kite-flying afternoons. It brings a quality of light — thin, golden, arriving at an angle not possible in the rest of the year — that painters and photographers have chased across the Bengal countryside for generations.

I spent the winter of 2018 moving between Kolkata and the districts of Murshidabad, Birbhum, and Hooghly, photographing the season as a phenomenon. I was not interested in a comprehensive account; I was interested in the specific textures of a particular kind of time. The photographs I made are not documentary records in any strict sense. They are attempts to hold a season that is already, before you have finished photographing it, beginning to recede.

What strikes me about Bengal winter is its quality of availability — everything comes at once, is fully present for a few weeks, and then is gone. The nolen gur, the saag, the morning mist on the Hooghly, the warm afternoon light that makes the mustard fields look lit from within: these are not background features but the season itself, made tangible in specific, perishable forms.

I am a photographer from the south of India. I came to Bengal with a curiosity that is also a form of cultural distance, and I was aware of that distance throughout the work. What I hope is that the distance produced clarity rather than misunderstanding — that seeing as an outsider, attentive and without assumptions, was useful rather than merely foreign.

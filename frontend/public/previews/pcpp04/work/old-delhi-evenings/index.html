Old Delhi Evenings

The last light in the lanes of Shahjahanabad

Old Delhi is a city that exists simultaneously in several centuries. In Chandni Chowk, you can buy silver filigree in a shop whose methods have not changed since the Mughal court demanded it, eat a biryani whose recipe is older than the British presence in India, and see the notifications from the municipal authority about the redevelopment plan that will, if implemented, demolish the gali you are standing in. The simultaneity is not comfortable. It is what makes Old Delhi one of the most photographically difficult places I know.

I spent two months in the evenings of Shahjahanabad — the walled city that Shah Jahan built in the 1640s and that has been modified, damaged, partially rebuilt, and comprehensively inhabited ever since. I photographed only in the evenings, from around four in the afternoon until dark, because the evening light in Old Delhi is unlike the light anywhere else I have photographed: it arrives at an angle that catches the ochre and red of the old buildings and turns the lanes briefly golden before everything goes dark and the sodium lamps take over.

What I was looking for was not the monument — not the Red Fort or the Jama Masjid, which have been photographed a million times — but the ordinary life that continues in their shadow. The kite-sellers and the scribes, the dyers and the Hakeems, the children playing in the lanes that were laid out in the seventeenth century: these are the people who give the city its living complexity, without whom it would be an open-air museum.

I left Old Delhi with a conviction that this is one of the places in the world that most repays slow, repeated attention. A week of visits produces photographs of surfaces. Two months in the evenings begins to produce something else — images from inside rather than from outside, made by someone who has, at least partially, learned to see what is being shown to them rather than what they expected to find.

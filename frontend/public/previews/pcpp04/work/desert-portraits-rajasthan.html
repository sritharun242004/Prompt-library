Desert Portraits

Faces of the Thar — the people who made the desert their home

The Thar is not an empty desert. This is the first thing one must unlearn. It is, in fact, one of the most densely populated arid zones on earth — home to communities of extraordinary cultural complexity, linguistic diversity, and craft sophistication, living in conditions that should not, by any reasonable ecological accounting, support this density of human life. The Thar's inhabitants have solved the problem of living in a desert not by engineering the desert into submission but by adapting to it with ingenuity accumulated over millennia.

I spent four months in the villages and small towns of western Rajasthan making portraits — not environmental portraits, not images designed to explain the landscape, but close-in photographs of individual faces. The Bishnoi woman in red, the Kalbeliya dancer, the Rabari herdsman: I was interested in specificity rather than type. Every person in this series was photographed in conversation, in the sense that I spent time with each of them before raising the camera.

The categories that organise this series — caste, community, craft — are imposed by me as a non-Rajasthani looking from outside. The people themselves experience their identities differently, as overlapping memberships in families, villages, professions, and religious traditions that the sociological categories do not fully capture. I offer the categories because the photographs need some frame of reference. They should be held loosely.

What the portraits are, finally, is a record of presence. These faces belong to communities that are being homogenised by development, tourism, and the steady inward flow of a national culture that does not know what to do with this degree of difference. I made these photographs because I did not want the difference to go undocumented.

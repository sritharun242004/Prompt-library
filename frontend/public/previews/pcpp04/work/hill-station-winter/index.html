Hill Station Winter

The off-season at Shimla, Darjeeling, and Ooty

The hill stations were not built for winter. The British who designed them — Shimla as summer capital, Darjeeling as retreat from the Calcutta heat, Ooty as ersatz England in the Nilgiris — envisioned them as seasonal migrations. In summer they were full; in winter they were abandoned. This logic has shaped everything: the architecture, the economy, the social life, the very idea of what these places are for.

I went in winter deliberately. I wanted to see the places without their intended population, which is to say without tourists, without the performance of heritage that the tourist economy requires. What I found was something quieter and more interesting: the communities that actually live in these places, for whom the hill station is not a destination but a home.

In Shimla, the closed hotels and the absent visitors revealed the structure of a town in which the permanent population — the shopkeepers, the caretakers, the civil servants, the schoolchildren — live in a kind of negotiation with a landscape that has been extensively designed for other people. In Darjeeling, the Tibetan community and the Gorkha workers who manage the tea estates occupy a town that was built by and for the Bengali and British tourist and has spent 150 years becoming something else entirely.

The photographs in this series make no argument. They are an attempt to see what remains when the season ends. What remains is, I think, more interesting than what the season brings.

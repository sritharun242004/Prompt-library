A Thousand Faces of Madurai

Street portraits from the temple city over four weeks

Madurai has been a city for at least 2,500 years, which means it has had 2,500 years to arrive at its current arrangement: dense, loud, jasmine-scented, politically contentious, and oriented around a temple complex that functions simultaneously as a religious centre, a tourist attraction, a neighbourhood landmark, and the city's psychological core. To photograph in Madurai is to photograph inside a city that has an extremely strong sense of itself.

I spent four weeks making street portraits in the old city — in the commercial streets around the Meenakshi temple, in the silk-merchant district, in the neighbourhoods where the auto-rickshaw drivers and flower sellers and construction workers live. I used a single 50mm lens and asked permission in most cases. Tamil-speaking strangers gave me a formal dignity in front of the camera that I was not always able to match.

The portraits in this series are not representative in any sociological sense. They are a collection of encounters — with a priest, a scholar, a napping driver, a woman drawing her kolam in the pre-dawn quiet — that happened to be photographed and that happened to be worth keeping. The selection is mine; the faces are theirs.

What strikes me now, looking back at these photographs, is how consistently the subjects met the camera with something other than self-consciousness. In Madurai, perhaps because the city is old enough to have accommodated many kinds of looking, the camera seemed less intrusive than usual. People looked back at me through the viewfinder with a directness and an ease that I associate with cities that know who they are.

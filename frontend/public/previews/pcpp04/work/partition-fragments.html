Partition Fragments

Objects, places, and the last witnesses of 1947

There is a specific kind of grief that belongs to the generation that experienced Partition: not the grief of war, which is acute and legible, but something slower and stranger — the grief of an ordinary life that was simply halted, and that could never quite be resumed. The people I photographed in 2020 were old, in their eighties and nineties, and the Partition they described was not primarily political. It was the loss of a neighbourhood, a school, a road, a particular quality of afternoon light falling on a particular courtyard in Lyallpur or Gujranwala.

I went looking for objects. The Partition Archive told me where to find the people who kept things from 1947 — the documents, the photographs, the pieces of property that had been carried across the border and preserved for seventy years, not because they were valuable, but because they were proof that the other place had existed. A lock with a key still in it. A letter in Sindhi. A photograph of a house. These objects are not memories; they are memory's anchors.

What struck me, interviewing people in their eighties and nineties, was the precision of their recollections and the inadequacy of any framework I brought to receive them. The historical categories — Hindu, Muslim, Sikh; India, Pakistan; victim, perpetrator — dissolved on contact with the actual accounts. People described Muslims who hid their Hindu neighbours and Hindus who betrayed theirs; Sikhs who crossed the border to rescue Muslim friends and Sikhs who committed atrocities in the same week. The moral picture was not complicated by these accounts. It was simply human.

The photographs in this series are made with this complexity in mind. I did not come to Amritsar or Jodhpur or the Wagah border with an argument. I came to document what remains — the objects, the landscapes, the faces of the last people who remember — before the last witnesses are gone and the archive closes.

City of Widows

The ashram women of Vrindavan — faith, abandonment, and the long wait

Vrindavan contains an argument about what a woman is permitted to be after her husband dies. In the traditional upper-caste Hindu framework — still operative in many of the communities from which the women who come here originate — a widow is a social non-entity: inauspicious, burdensome, stripped of every marker of wifely status. The white saree is not mourning dress; it is a uniform of erasure. Some families send their widowed mothers or mothers-in-law to Vrindavan because it is the godly thing to do. Others send them because they cannot think of anything else to do with them.

I spent three months in Vrindavan in 2022, working primarily in four ashrams and on the Yamuna ghats. The women I met ranged in age from twenty-nine to ninety-four. They came from West Bengal, Bangladesh, Odisha, Rajasthan, and Uttar Pradesh. They spoke different languages and came from different castes. What they shared was this: they had all arrived in Vrindavan because their families had no place for them, and they had all, in varying degrees and by different routes, found something here.

I want to be careful about what I say that something is. It is not a resolution. It is not happiness, or at least not simply happiness. It is a form of life that has been made available in a town that has chosen, for reasons of its own theology, to welcome women who have nowhere else to go. The bhajans, the Yamuna baths, the rhythm of prayer and meal and sleep — these constitute a structure within which even the most desolate find a reason to wake up at four in the morning.

Whether this is beautiful or terrible or both is a question I leave to the photographs. I photographed for three months and emerged with no conclusions and with a kind of awe that I am still not sure how to parse. The women of Vrindavan are not victims, or not only that. They are people who have been given an impossible situation and made something of it. That something deserves documentation and, I think, a deeper silence than commentary can provide.

Salt and Ceremony

The vanishing salt pans of the Rann of Kutch

Every October, when the monsoon withdraws and the great white desert hardens again, the Agaria families of Kutch pack their utensils, their children, and their uncertain futures onto trucks headed for the Rann. They will not return to their villages until June. In between, they live on a surface that is neither land nor sea — a geological liminal zone where salt has been harvested for at least eight centuries.

The Rann of Kutch is the largest salt desert in the world, and the people who work it have been doing so in the same way their ancestors did under the Mughal revenue rolls. The brine is pumped from beneath the surface into shallow rectangular pans, where over months of evaporation and crystallisation, salt slowly forms. It is not a dramatic process. It is the kind of work that wears itself into the body — into cracked feet and damaged eyes and lungs abraded by salt dust — without ceremony or acknowledgement.

I spent four months with three Agaria families during the 2023–24 season. What I found was not the photogenic poverty that photojournalism traffics in, but something stranger and more ordinary: a community engaged in the most ancient of human crafts, fully aware of its precarity, carrying on because there is nothing else and because, for reasons that resist economic analysis, they choose it. The children go to school in the nearest town three days a week. The older men talk about cricket. The women gossip and sing.

When I asked Ramji Bhai, who has worked salt for forty-two years, whether he would do it again given the choice, he looked at me for a long moment before answering. "The salt knows us," he said. I wrote it down without understanding it. I think I understand it now.

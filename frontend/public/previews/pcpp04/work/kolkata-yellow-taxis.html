The Yellow Taxis

Before the last Ambassador disappears from Kolkata streets

The Ambassador was manufactured between 1958 and 2014, which is to say it had a production run longer than the Soviet Union and the moon programme. It is a slightly modified version of the 1948 Morris Oxford, which means that every yellow taxi in Kolkata is a vehicle designed for post-war Britain, assembled in Uttarpara, and still, somehow, running. The mechanics who keep them alive are the most resourceful problem-solvers I have ever met.

I spent two months in Kolkata in 2022 photographing the yellow taxi community — drivers, owners, mechanics, the tea stall wallahs who set up near taxi stands — as part of a longer investigation into things that are ending. The question I began with was simple: what happens to a city when its most iconic image disappears? I did not arrive at a simple answer.

What I found was a community that was not so much dying as disaggregating. Some drivers have moved to app-based platforms. Some have sold their licences and returned to their districts. Some are keeping their Ambassadors alive through sheer stubbornness and the genius of Kolkata mechanics who can machine a replacement part for a discontinued Morris Oxford component at two in the morning for four hundred rupees. The cab as an institution is not ending; it is being renegotiated.

The photographs in this series are not elegies. I resist the elegiac mode in documentary photography because it tends to aestheticise decline in ways that serve the photographer and not the subject. What I wanted to make were portraits of people engaged in the ordinary business of their work — driving, waiting, talking, sleeping — who happen to be doing so in vehicles that the rest of the world has decided are obsolete.

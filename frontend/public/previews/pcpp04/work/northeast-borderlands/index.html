Northeast Borderlands

The contested margins of Nagaland, Manipur, and Mizoram

The northeast of India is not one place. It is eight states, dozens of major tribes, hundreds of communities, and an approximation of forty languages, operating under a set of political arrangements that were largely designed by the central government without extensive consultation with the people most affected by them. To call this region "marginal" is to accept the logic of New Delhi — a logic that the peoples of Nagaland, Manipur, and Mizoram have spent decades resisting, negotiating, and sometimes violently rejecting.

I travelled through three of the eight states over three months in 2019. I had access that most outside photographers do not, through introductions from Naga and Mizo journalists who were generous with their networks. What I encountered was a complexity that resists the political categories typically applied to it: not simply restive minorities seeking independence, not simply an Indian state project of integration, but communities in the middle of the long, slow, painful work of deciding who they are in relation to a national framework they did not choose.

The photographs I made are portraits of ordinary life in extraordinary political circumstances. The woman weaving at her loin-loom, the pastor in his church, the children on the mountain path: none of these images are directly about the Armed Forces Special Powers Act or the various peace agreements or the political demands of the Naga nationalist movement. But all of them are shaped by these facts, the way all photographs of people are shaped by the structures within which those people live.

I offer these photographs with a particular tentativeness. This is not my region, not my culture, not my political struggle. I have tried to be a witness rather than an interpreter, and to let the photographs carry the weight of the looking without imposing a meaning that the subjects themselves might not recognise.

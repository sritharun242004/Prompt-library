Backwaters

The Kuttanad lowlands — farming below sea level

Kuttanad is an argument with gravity. The paddy fields of this Kerala lowland sit between 0.6 and 2.2 metres below sea level — a geographical anomaly that has been exploited by generations of farmers who built a network of bunds, canals, and water-scoop mechanisms sophisticated enough to grow two rice crops a year in a landscape that should, by rights, be underwater. The Dutch have polders; the Venetians have lagoons. The people of Kuttanad have the backwaters.

I spent two months in the Alappuzha district during the 2020 Kharif season, sleeping in a village that was accessible only by boat and watching an agricultural system of extraordinary complexity function with an efficiency that has not changed in its essentials since the nineteenth century. The mechanisation is superficial: electric pumps where wooden water-scoops once stood, outboard motors on the boats. The knowledge — of soil, water, weather, the particular behaviour of this landscape — remains deeply embodied and largely oral.

The threat, in 2020, was twofold. The first was climate: the monsoons had become less predictable, the sea-level was rising, and the salinity of Vembanad Lake — which provides the region's water — was increasing as the barriers that once protected it from the Arabian Sea were eroded. The second was economic: rice farming in Kuttanad had become marginal, with the cost of maintaining the pumps and bunds exceeding the return from the crop in bad years. The young were leaving. The old were asking whether it was worth continuing.

Whether Kuttanad's farming system survives the century is an open question. The photographs in this series do not argue for or against continuation; they document the system as it existed in 2020, in full operation, managed by people who understood it completely and were uncertain of its future. That uncertainty is present in every image, even when what is pictured is as simple as a woman cutting rice in standing water at dawn.

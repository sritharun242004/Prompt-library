How I Built My First Info Product in 30 Days

From blank Notion doc to $12,000 launch — a complete post-mortem on building a course from zero, including the mistakes I made on day one.

The month I launched my first course, I had 2,300 newsletter subscribers, no audience on any other platform, and zero experience with course platforms. The launch generated $12,000. This is everything I did, including the mistakes.

Day 1: Choosing the wrong topic

My first idea was to teach the Zettelkasten note-taking method. I had used it for two years. I had a lot to say. I spent four days outlining the curriculum before I asked a single person if they would buy it.

No one wanted it. Or rather, the people who were interested already had free resources and weren't willing to pay for more. This is mistake number one: validating the idea in your head before validating it in the market.

Day 5: Starting over with pre-validation

I posted a question to my newsletter: "What's the most frustrating thing about building an audience online?" I got 87 replies. The most common answer was a variation of: "I know what to write but I can't make myself consistent."

That was the product. Not a note-taking system. A consistency system. I replied to every person who responded and asked if they'd pay $49 for a structured 4-week program that solved it. 23 people said yes. I had pre-sold the course before building a single lesson.

Day 8: Building the minimum viable curriculum

I did not use a course platform. I recorded Loom videos, dropped them in Notion, and charged for Notion access. The production quality was deliberately rough. I wanted signal on whether the content was valuable, not whether the platform was polished.

Day 28: The launch

I emailed my list. No countdown timers. No scarcity tactics. A plain email that said: "I built a thing. Here's who it's for. Here's what it costs. Here's the link." The email was 300 words.

$12,000 in 72 hours. Mostly from people who had pre-validated in week one.

What I would do differently

Pre-validate earlier. Build even rougher. Launch to fewer people and get more feedback. The 30-day timeline created artificial pressure that pushed me to skip steps I should not have skipped. The thing that worked was the pre-validation, not the timeline.

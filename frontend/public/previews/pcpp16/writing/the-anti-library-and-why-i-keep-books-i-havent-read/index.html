The Anti-Library and Why I Keep Books I Haven't Read

Nassim Taleb's concept of the anti-library — a collection of books you own but haven't read — is one of the most useful frameworks for intellectual humility.

Nassim Taleb describes the anti-library: the books on your shelf that you have not read are more valuable than the ones you have, because they represent what you do not know.

Most people build libraries as monuments to what they've read. The goal is to fill all the shelves and feel the completeness. But the books you've already read no longer surprise you. They can reinforce and refine, but the real value of a library is as a map of available knowledge — including the territory you haven't explored yet.

The intellectual humility function

I maintain approximately 400 books in my physical library. I've read, by conservative estimate, about 150 of them. The unread 250 are not a source of guilt. They are a constant reminder that I know a fraction of what's knowable in the domains I care about.

This is an anti-library in Taleb's sense. The sight of books I haven't read keeps me epistemically humble in a way that a shelf of completed books never could. Every unread spine is a question I haven't yet answered.

The practical function

The anti-library also functions as an on-demand resource. When I encounter a problem or question, I often already own the relevant book. I bought "The Innovator's Dilemma" two years before I needed to think about competitive strategy. When I needed it, it was there. The opportunity cost of buying books you don't immediately read is low. The option value of having them available is real.

The buying heuristic

I buy books when I am curious about the domain, not when I need a specific answer. Buying to answer a specific question often produces unsatisfying results — the book is rarely organized around your exact question. Buying out of curiosity means the book sits until the moment you need exactly what it has. That moment almost always comes.

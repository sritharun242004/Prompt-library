Protein Optimization for Knowledge Workers

Cognitive performance is downstream of protein intake in ways most desk workers don't appreciate. Here is the data and my current protocol.

Most productivity writing treats the brain as if it floats in a vacuum. Optimize your calendar, your environment, your habits. Rarely: optimize what you feed it.

Protein is the most underappreciated lever for knowledge workers. Not because of muscle protein synthesis — that's the gym crowd's framing. Because of neurotransmitter precursors.

The biochemistry (briefly)

Dopamine, serotonin, and norepinephrine — the neurotransmitters most relevant to focus, motivation, and mood — are synthesized from amino acids. Tyrosine and phenylalanine produce dopamine and norepinephrine. Tryptophan produces serotonin. All three come from dietary protein.

When protein intake is insufficient, neurotransmitter production is constrained at the supply side. You can have perfect sleep, a perfect environment, and a perfect schedule, but if you're running a deficit on the raw materials for dopamine synthesis, focus will be blunt.

What the data says

The Recommended Daily Allowance for protein is 0.8g per kilogram of body weight. This is the minimum to prevent deficiency, not the amount to optimize performance. Research on cognitive function and protein intake consistently suggests that 1.6–2.2g per kg produces meaningfully better outcomes on sustained attention and executive function tasks.

For a 75kg person, that is 120–165g per day. The average American adult consumes approximately 88g. The gap is significant.

My current protocol

I aim for 160g per day, spread across four eating occasions. I don't use protein powder as a primary source — it misses the micronutrients that come with whole food protein. My four primary sources: eggs (morning), Greek yogurt (mid-morning), a substantial meat or fish at lunch, and a legume or dairy-based protein at dinner.

The cognitive difference, subjectively, is noticeable within 10 days of hitting consistent targets. I cannot prove causation in an n=1, but the signal is consistent enough that I've maintained the protocol for 14 months.

The One-Sentence Rule for Clearer Writing

Before writing anything — email, essay, tweet — write one sentence that summarizes the entire piece. If you can't, you're not ready to write it yet.

The most common cause of unclear writing is unclear thinking. Not bad vocabulary. Not poor grammar. Unclear thinking manifested as words on a page.

The one-sentence rule is a diagnostic tool I've used for four years. Before writing anything longer than a paragraph, I write one sentence that contains: the main claim, who it's for, and why it matters.

If I cannot write that sentence, I stop. Not because I don't have things to say — I usually have too many. But because the inability to produce the sentence reveals something: I don't actually know what I'm trying to say yet. I'm using the writing as a way to figure it out, which is fine for a journal but produces unreadable drafts.

The format

The sentence takes this form: "For [audience], this piece argues [claim], because [why it matters]."

For my morning routine essay: "For people who have tried and abandoned productivity routines, this piece argues that the problem is mismatched biology, not lack of discipline, because understanding the cortisol awakening response lets you design a routine that works with your body instead of against it."

That sentence does not appear in the finished essay. But every paragraph of the essay serves it. When I write a paragraph that doesn't, I cut it.

The editing application

The one-sentence rule is most powerful as an editing tool. After a first draft, write the sentence again — not from your outline but from what you actually wrote. If the sentence changes, your draft changed direction on you. This is information. Either revise the draft to match the original sentence, or update the sentence and revise the draft to match the new intent.

Most essays that feel unfocused have this problem: the writer started with one idea and ended up somewhere else, and neither beginning nor end is sharp as a result.

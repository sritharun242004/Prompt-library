The Craft of the Newsletter Essay

Newsletter essays are their own form — shorter than a book chapter, longer than a post, personal but argued. Here is how to write them well.

The newsletter essay is a genre. Not a blog post. Not a personal essay. Not journalism. Something in between — a form that has been developed, somewhat accidentally, by writers who needed to produce weekly substance for paying audiences.

The best practitioners of the form — Paul Graham, Morgan Housel, Robin Sloan, Mandy Brown — have developed specific techniques for the genre's constraints. Here is what I've extracted from their work and from writing 80+ of them myself.

The personal example as proof

In longer nonfiction, you can cite research, statistics, and expert opinion as your primary evidence. In the newsletter essay, these feel imported — borrowed gravitas. The most convincing evidence in this form is personal experience, used specifically and honestly.

The technique: make a claim. Then support it with a specific experience, not a general one. Not "I've noticed that..." but "In October 2023, when I was building my second course, I tried X and the result was Y." The specificity is the credibility.

The question as opener

The best newsletter essays begin with a question or an anomaly — something that doesn't fit, something the reader will recognize as true and also recognize as unresolved. This creates an implicit contract: the essay will address the question or explain the anomaly. The reader knows what they signed up for.

Avoid beginning with context, history, or definition. These are delay tactics — you're not ready to write yet, so you're warming up on the page and asking the reader to wait.

The landing

The newsletter essay needs to land. Not resolve — essays that resolve everything leave nothing for the reader to carry. But land: the end should feel earned, not like the writer ran out of things to say. The technique is to return to the opening question or image, answer it partially, and leave one thread deliberately open. The open thread becomes what the reader thinks about after.

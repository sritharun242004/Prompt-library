How to Price Your First Course (Without Guessing)

Pricing information products is not intuitive. Here is the framework I used to price three courses, including the psychological levers that actually matter.

The most common mistake first-time course creators make is pricing based on what they would pay, not what the problem is worth to the buyer.

Your personal willingness to pay for educational content is not a valid signal. You already know what's in the course. The buyer is paying to acquire knowledge, skills, or an outcome — and the price should be calibrated to the value of that outcome, not the hours of content or the cost of production.

The value-based pricing framework

Start with the outcome your course produces. Not the content ("12 hours of video and a workbook") — the outcome ("the ability to build a deployed AI application from scratch"). Then estimate the economic value of that outcome to your target buyer.

For someone who wants to build an AI product to sell, the economic value is potentially large. For someone who wants to understand AI enough to work more effectively at their current job, the value is lower but still meaningful. You are not setting a price for all possible buyers — you are setting a price for the buyer you are targeting.

The three-price test

Before launching, pick three price points: one you think is too low, one you think is right, and one that feels audacious. Show these to 10 people who fit your target buyer profile and ask: "At what price would this be a no-brainer? At what price would you think it was suspiciously cheap? At what price would you say it's too expensive?"

The answers cluster in ways that tell you where the market is. The "suspiciously cheap" threshold is important — it reveals the minimum acceptable price for the category, below which buyers assume the content is low quality.

What I learned pricing three courses

My first course was priced at $49. It sold well but I later raised it to $149 with no change in conversion rate, which told me I had underpriced. My second course launched at $199. Conversion was lower but revenue per student was higher and the students were more engaged. My third launched at $299 and sold to a smaller, more committed audience with the highest completion rate of all three.

The lesson: higher prices attract more committed buyers, which improves completion rates, which improves testimonials, which improves future sales. Underpricing hurts the product's reputation as much as its revenue.

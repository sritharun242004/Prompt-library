5 Ways to Fix Your Sleep Without Supplements

Before reaching for melatonin, magnesium, or anything else with a label, try these five environmental and behavioral changes that actually work.

The sleep supplement market generated $11 billion in 2024. Most of it was spent on products that address symptoms rather than causes.

Poor sleep, in the majority of cases, is an environmental and behavioral problem before it is a nutritional one. Here are five changes that cost nothing and have more evidence behind them than most supplements.

1. Consistent wake time (not bedtime)

The circadian rhythm is anchored to wake time, not bedtime. If you wake at the same time every day — including weekends — your body learns to build sleep pressure consistently. Most people focus on what time they go to bed. The more powerful lever is what time they get up.

2. Light in the first 30 minutes

Bright light exposure within 30 minutes of waking sets the circadian clock and, 14–16 hours later, triggers appropriate melatonin release. This is not about being outside — it is about getting bright light (2000+ lux, which outdoor light provides naturally) during the cortisol awakening window. On cloudy days, a 10,000 lux light therapy lamp achieves the same effect.

3. Temperature drop before bed

Core body temperature drops approximately 1–2°C in the hour before natural sleep onset. You can accelerate this by making your sleeping environment slightly cool (65–68°F / 18–20°C), taking a warm bath 90 minutes before bed (the rebound cooling accelerates the drop), or both.

4. No alcohol within 3 hours of bed

Alcohol is a sedative, not a sleep aid. It produces unconsciousness but suppresses REM sleep, which is the phase responsible for emotional processing and memory consolidation. The sedation feels like sleep but the architecture is disrupted. Even two drinks reliably degrades sleep quality by a measurable margin.

5. The cognitive shuffle

If racing thoughts are the problem, try the cognitive shuffle before reaching for anything. Think of an unrelated object — a giraffe, a hammock, a blender. Then form a random, slightly nonsensical image. Then another. The randomness interrupts the narrative processing that keeps you awake. It works for approximately 80% of people who try it with intention.

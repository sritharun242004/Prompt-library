I Read 100 Books in a Year. Here's What It Taught Me About Reading.

The year I read 100 books, I realized I had been doing it for the wrong reasons. Reading volume is a vanity metric. Reading depth is the actual goal.

The year I read 100 books was the year I stopped reading for the count.

The first three months, I was proud of the pace. By month six, I was gaming it — choosing short books, skimming the padded ones, rushing conclusions so I could mark them done. By month nine, when I reviewed my notes, I realized most of the books I'd "read" had left no residue. I had processed them like content, not like instruction.

The productivity trap

There is a trap in productivity culture around reading: reading is treated as inherently virtuous, so more reading is more virtuous, so maximizing reading is the goal. This is backwards. Reading is instrumental to understanding. If reading more reduces understanding, you are running the wrong optimization.

The books that changed how I think, I read slowly. Sometimes twice. I argued with them in the margins. I wrote notes that were twice as long as the chapter. I stopped after important passages and thought about whether they were true, in my experience, before continuing. This is not efficient. It is effective.

What I did differently the year after

I set a limit of 30 books. I chose fewer, more difficult books. I read everything twice: once quickly to get the architecture, once slowly to get the ideas. I processed every book with a synthesis note within 48 hours of finishing. And I kept a separate document of "live questions" — questions the books raised that I hadn't resolved yet, which became the seeds of my writing.

The year I read 30 books, I retained and applied more than the year I read 100.

The question to ask

Before adding a book to your list: "What question am I trying to answer?" If you can't articulate the question, the book is not for you right now. The best reading is targeted to a live problem or curiosity. The worst reading is reading because you feel like you should.

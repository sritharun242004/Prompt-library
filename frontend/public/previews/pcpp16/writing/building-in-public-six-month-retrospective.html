Building in Public: A Six-Month Retrospective

What actually happened when I shared everything — revenue, failures, audience size, and the specific posts that drove growth. Unfiltered numbers inside.

Six months ago I committed to building in public. I would share revenue, failures, subscriber counts, and everything in between, once a month, no spin.

Here is what happened.

Month 1: $2,100 MRR, 1,847 subscribers

The first transparency post got 4x my average open rate. People were hungry for real numbers, not aspirational case studies. I shared that I had 1,847 subscribers and was making $2,100 per month, mostly from a course. Engagement was high. Revenue didn't move.

Month 3: $5,400 MRR, 3,200 subscribers

The transparency posts compounded. Being honest about Month 1 and Month 2 created trust that converted into purchases. The readers who had been lurking for months started buying. The most effective post was one where I shared a failed product launch: I built a Notion template, priced it at $29, and sold 3 copies. I wrote honestly about why it failed. That post drove 800 new subscribers in a week.

Month 6: $9,800 MRR, 5,400 subscribers

The audience grew 192% in six months. Revenue grew 367%. The correlation is not coincidental, but it's not as simple as "be transparent = make money." The mechanism is: transparency creates trust, trust converts to purchases, purchases create social proof, social proof attracts more subscribers.

What I would not do again

Sharing specific revenue figures attracted a small number of people who were interested only in replicating the numbers, not the work. They subscribed, extracted what they could, and unsubscribed. Churn from this cohort was notably higher.

What worked better than expected

Sharing failures. Not as content strategy but genuinely — documenting the real experience of things not working. People subscribe to content they find useful. They trust content that feels honest. Failures feel honest because they are.

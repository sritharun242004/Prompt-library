How I Became a Developer: A Self-Taught Story

September 3, 20223 min read
  • career
  • learning

I didn't go to school for computer science. I have a degree in something completely unrelated, and I stumbled into programming through a combination of curiosity and frustration at not being able to build the things I had in my head.

This is the path I took.

The Beginning

I started with HTML and CSS — the natural entry point for most self-taught developers. I wanted to build a website, so I learned what I needed to build a website.

The first moment I felt like a real programmer was when I figured out how to make a dropdown menu without a tutorial. I'd been following guides step by step, but this time I worked it out myself from the docs and a lot of experimentation. It sounds trivial but it was significant — I understood something I hadn't been told.

How I Actually Learned

I learned by building things. Not by finishing courses or earning certificates. I would pick a project — something I actually wanted to exist — and then learn whatever I needed to build it.

The projects mattered. When you have a real goal, stuck means something. You're not stuck on a tutorial exercise. You're stuck on your own thing, and that's much more motivating to push through.

I also read a lot. Not textbooks — blog posts. Other developers writing about what they'd figured out. This led to writing my own blog posts: the act of writing forced me to understand things at a level where I could explain them.

What I'd Do Differently

I spent too long on tutorials. The comfortable feeling of following along is not learning. You think you understand because someone is holding your hand, but remove the scaffolding and you're lost.

I'd spend less time on courses and more time on documentation. MDN for web APIs. The React docs. The TypeScript handbook. Official docs are dense but accurate, and learning to read them is itself a skill.

I'd also start building in public earlier. The fear of showing imperfect work kept me from getting feedback that would have accelerated everything.

The Job

I got my first developer job through a blog post. Someone read something I wrote about CSS, reached out, and eventually hired me. The writing was both a learning tool and a portfolio.

It's a common path for self-taught developers. The output of learning — the blog posts, the open-source contributions, the side projects — becomes evidence that you can do the work.

One Thing That's True

Programming is a craft you get better at by doing it. The learning never stops — it just changes. Early on, you're learning basic syntax and patterns. Later, you're learning architecture and tradeoffs and when not to use a particular technology. The mindset is the same: build something, figure out what you don't know, learn that thing, repeat.

The self-taught path is real. It's not easier than school, but it's available to anyone with internet access and time.

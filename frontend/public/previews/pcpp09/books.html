Books Reviewed

We read books slowly and write about them the same way. These are not summaries or star ratings — they are attempts to think alongside a book and to invite others into the conversation.

Book Review

Braiding Sweetgrass

by Robin Wall Kimmerer · 2013

Kimmerer brings to botany what Woolf brought to the novel: a sensibility so alert to the life of things that the inanimate world becomes, under her attention, thoroughly animated. This is science writing at its most literary and literature at its most scientific.

Robin Wall Kimmerer is a botanist and a member of the Citizen Potawatomi Nation, and Braiding Sweetgrass is the record of what happens when those two identities are allowed to speak to each other without one silencing the other. The result is a book unlike any I have read: part natural history, part memoir, part philosophy of reciprocity, written in prose that sustains its quality across nearly four hundred pages.

The central argument of the book — that Western science's insistence on objectivity has cost us something essential in our relationship to the natural world — will be familiar to readers of certain strains of environmental philosophy. What is not familiar is the gentleness and specificity with which Kimmerer advances it. She is not polemical; she is observant. The argument emerges from the particulars of sweetgrass and asters, of maple sugar and wild strawberries, of traditional ecological knowledge that has survived centuries of suppression.

What lingers is the grammar of animacy — Kimmerer's meditation on languages that assign personhood to rivers and mosses, and what we lose when we speak of nature as "it." This is not sentimentalism; it is a serious claim about the relationship between language and perception, about how the words we have available determine what we are able to notice. To read this book is to begin to hear, even in English, the voices that Kimmerer hears in her grandmother's Potawatomi.

Book Review

Ways of Seeing

by John Berger · 1972

Few books have changed more minds about how to look at pictures. Berger's prose is deceptively plain, his arguments deceptively simple — and the more you look at the images he discusses, the more you understand how radical a book this is.

John Berger began Ways of Seeing as a television series for the BBC, and it retains the television series' virtues: directness, pace, a refusal to patronize, an insistence on being understood. These are virtues that art criticism does not always possess. Berger believed that the way we see things is affected by what we know or what we believe, and he set out to demonstrate this not by abstract argument but by looking at specific images and asking what assumptions we bring to them.

The most famous essay in the book — on the nude in European oil painting — remains, half a century later, the most efficient demolition of a set of cultural assumptions I have ever read. In fewer than thirty pages, Berger makes visible the male gaze as a structural feature of a tradition, embedded not in individual works but in the conventions of the tradition itself. The woman in the painting is arranged for a spectator who is assumed to be male. This is not an accusation; it is an observation. The accusation comes from seeing how rarely the observation has been made.

The book is also about the relationship between original works of art and their mechanical reproduction — a dialogue with Benjamin's essay on the same subject, conducted in plainer language. Berger's point is subtle: reproduction is not a simple diminishment of the original but a transformation of it, a creation of a new kind of object with different uses and different meanings. The postcard of the Mona Lisa is not a lesser Mona Lisa; it is a different thing, with its own cultural life.

Book Review

A Pattern Language

by Christopher Alexander · 1977

Alexander's great book is ostensibly about architecture and urban design but is actually about the conditions under which human beings flourish — about the patterns, at every scale from the city to the window seat, that make life liveable.

Christopher Alexander spent his career studying what makes buildings and places feel alive — what gives some spaces the quality of being inhabited, animated, genuinely welcoming, and leaves others feeling dead despite their technical adequacy. A Pattern Language is the systematic result of this inquiry: 253 patterns, ranging in scale from the regional ("Agricultural valleys") to the intimate ("A place to wait"), each described in terms of the forces it reconciles and the human need it serves.

The book is best read slowly, one pattern at a time, allowing each to settle into your understanding of the spaces you inhabit. You begin to see the patterns around you — the window seat that catches afternoon light, the half-open door that indicates welcome, the place at the edge of a public square where one can sit and watch without being seen. These are not arbitrary design choices; they are responses to deep human needs for shelter, prospect, community, and solitude that Alexander has worked out from first principles and empirical observation.

What makes A Pattern Language a book for readers rather than merely a manual for architects is the quality of attention Alexander brings to the question of what it means to feel at home somewhere. The book's underlying argument is that beauty in the built environment is not arbitrary but arises from the resolution of actual human needs — that the beautiful building and the good building are, in the end, the same building. This is an argument with implications that extend far beyond architecture.

Book Review

The Rings of Saturn

by W. G. Sebald · 1995

Sebald's walk through Suffolk becomes a meditation on ruin, memory, and the slow erasure of everything that ever mattered. No other writer of the last century has found a form adequate to this subject — or has made it so beautiful to contemplate.

W. G. Sebald took a walking tour of Suffolk in 1992 and emerged, a year later, hospitalized for a condition diagnosed as "almost total immobility and paralysis." The Rings of Saturn is the account of that walk — and of the history, memory, and association that the landscape provoked. It is one of the strangest and most beautiful books of the twentieth century, and it is impossible to describe without making it sound both more systematic and more mystical than it actually is.

The book's method is digression elevated to form: a ruined manor suggests the decline of an aristocratic family which suggests the history of British colonialism in China which suggests the silk trade which suggests Thomas Browne's meditation on patterns in nature which suggests the narrator's childhood memory of a particular shade of light. The connections are associative rather than logical, but they accumulate into something that functions like an argument: a sustained meditation on the way that beauty and devastation are inseparable, that every lovely thing contains its own undoing.

The photographs embedded in the text — blurred, slightly sinister, never quite matching their captions — contribute to the book's characteristic atmosphere of uncertain documentation. We are never quite sure what is remembered and what is imagined, what is historical fact and what is Sebald's private mythology. This uncertainty is the book's deepest form. What Sebald is investigating is the nature of memory itself — its faithlessness, its beauty, its tendency to preserve what should have been forgotten and forget what should have been preserved.

Book Review

The Examined Life

by Stephen Grosz · 2013

In fifty brief chapters, psychoanalyst Stephen Grosz offers fifty case studies that are also fifty meditations on the human capacity for self-deception — and on what it costs us to see ourselves clearly.

Stephen Grosz has been a practicing psychoanalyst in London for more than twenty-five years. The Examined Life is a collection of stories from that practice, each one as tightly constructed as a short story and as honestly observed as the best journalism. What makes the book extraordinary is Grosz's refusal to editorialize: the stories are presented with the minimum of interpretation, and the reader is left to draw their own conclusions from the patterns that emerge.

The recurring theme — not stated but enacted — is the degree to which we are opaque to ourselves, how little we understand our own motivations, how faithfully we reproduce in adult life the dynamics of childhood even when (especially when) we believe we have escaped them. One patient spends years in analysis before understanding why she cannot allow herself to succeed; another discovers, in the analysis of a single dream, the origin of a lifelong pattern of self-sabotage. These are not cautionary tales; they are maps of the interior, drawn with great precision and compassion.

Grosz is a beautiful writer. His sentences are clear and exact, without the jargon that psychoanalytic writing so often defaults to. He has learned from his patients — from the precision with which they, when they are listening to themselves carefully, choose their words — and this learning shows in the quality of his prose. The book is short enough to read in a few hours and long enough to think about for years.

Book Review

Pilgrim at Tinker Creek

by Annie Dillard · 1974

Dillard spent a year beside a creek in Virginia's Blue Ridge Mountains and wrote one of the most extraordinary books in the American literary tradition — a work of attention so sustained it becomes, eventually, a form of prayer.

Annie Dillard was twenty-seven when she spent a year at Tinker Creek, keeping the journal that became this book. She was watching, primarily — learning, as she put it, to see. The book that emerged from this year of watching is not a nature book in any ordinary sense; it is a sustained act of attention that uses nature as its occasion and philosophy as its method, producing something that defies easy categorization and rewards repeated reading.

The book's great passage — the description of a frog deflated by a giant water bug that has inserted its beak and sucked out the frog's body — is often quoted as evidence of Dillard's willingness to look at what is difficult. But what surrounds this passage is equally important: the meditation on violence in nature not as aberration but as norm, on the way in which the natural world is structured by consumption at every level, on what it means to find beauty in a world that is also this. Dillard does not resolve this tension; she inhabits it.

What makes Pilgrim at Tinker Creek lastingly valuable is its quality of moral attention — the sense that something is genuinely at stake in the act of looking, that seeing clearly is not merely an aesthetic achievement but an ethical one. Dillard believes, or the book believes, that we are obligated to attend to the world as it actually is, in all its violence and beauty, and that this attending is itself a form of respect for what exists. This is not a comfortable position, and she does not make it comfortable. But it is a serious one.

Book Review

The Need for Roots

by Simone Weil · 1949

Written at the end of Weil's short life, this urgent meditation on the conditions of human flourishing anticipates debates we are still having. She argues that uprootedness — the severing of people from their communities and traditions — is the gravest disease of our time.

Simone Weil died in 1943, at thirty-four, of tuberculosis complicated by self-imposed starvation. The Need for Roots was written in the final months of her life at the request of Charles de Gaulle, who wanted a philosophical basis for the reconstruction of France after the war. Weil produced something both more practical and more radical than he had asked for: a comprehensive analysis of the conditions under which human beings can live well, and a diagnosis of the forms of uprootedness that modern life produces.

Weil's central argument is that human beings have a need for roots — for membership in a community that carries memory, tradition, and a sense of obligation across generations — that is as fundamental as the need for food or shelter. When this need is unmet, people do not simply feel lonely; they become susceptible to the compensatory certainties of ideology, to the totalitarian movements that offer the warmth of belonging at the cost of the self that belongs. Uprootedness, for Weil, is not metaphor but diagnosis.

The book is difficult in places — Weil was a philosopher and a mystic, and she does not always pause to explain her terms. But the difficulty is worth the effort, because the questions Weil is asking are precisely the questions that seem most urgent in our own moment: What do we owe to the communities that formed us? What is lost when tradition is severed? How do we build institutions that nourish rather than uproot? She does not provide easy answers. She provides something better: the right questions, asked with fierce clarity.

Book Review

The Art of the Personal Essay

by Phillip Lopate (ed.) · 1994

Lopate's anthology is the great introduction to the essayistic tradition — from Montaigne through Baldwin, from Lamb through Woolf. His introduction alone is worth the price: the best brief account of what the essay is and why it matters.

Phillip Lopate has spent his career defending and practicing the personal essay, and this anthology is both a summa of that defense and a demonstration of its warrant. He has gathered seventy-five essays from four centuries, arranged chronologically from Montaigne to our contemporaries, and prefaced them with an introduction that is itself an exemplary piece of essayistic thinking — personal, digressive, firmly argued, generously cited.

The introduction's key claim is that the personal essay is characterized not by its subject matter but by its tone: a mixture of honesty, doubt, and curiosity that Lopate traces back to Montaigne's willingness to take himself as his own primary subject of inquiry. This is not narcissism; it is method. The self is the instrument of investigation, and its quirks and limitations are part of the data. The essayist who pretends to an objectivity they do not possess has already failed at the form's most basic requirement.

The anthology itself is a generous education. Readers who know Montaigne but not Hazlitt, who know Woolf but not Lamb, who know Baldwin but not E. B. White, will find here exactly the breadth of reading that the form requires. Lopate's selections are eccentric in the best sense — personal, debatable, full of surprises. The book rewards sequential reading but also browsing; it is the kind of anthology one returns to for decades, always finding something previously missed.

Making as Meaning

The act of creation as a form of self-discovery

November 5, 2024·8 min read

E. M. Forster is supposed to have asked: How do I know what I think until I see what I say? The question is usually cited as a charming paradox, but it describes the actual phenomenology of creative work with more accuracy than most philosophical accounts. The painter who knows exactly what the canvas will look like before making a single mark is not a painter but a printer. The genuine creative act is characterized precisely by its openness to discovery — the possibility that the work will know something the maker does not.

This is why artists so often speak of being surprised by their own work, of characters who refuse to behave as planned, of arguments that lead to conclusions opposite from those intended. The psychologist D. W. Winnicott wrote about what he called the transitional space — the space between the inner world and the outer, where play and creativity happen. In this space, the usual boundary between self and world becomes permeable. We are neither fully in control nor fully out of it. The work happens in the gap.

This is why artists so often speak of being surprised by their own work, of characters who refuse to behave as planned, of arguments that lead to conclusions opposite from those intended.

This has implications for how we understand meaning in art. If meaning were simply encoded by the artist and decoded by the viewer, the process would be mechanical and reproducible. But the experience of genuine art does not feel like decoding. It feels like encounter — a meeting of the reader or viewer with something that exceeds them, that knows more than they do. This excess is not mystical. It is the residue of the creative process itself: the traces of discovery that remain in the finished work like marks of a passage.

To make something seriously is to accept that you will be changed by the process. The essay that began as an argument may end as a question. The sculpture may reveal a grief you did not know you were carrying. This is not inefficiency or failure of planning — it is the very purpose of making. We create not to express what we already know but to become capable of knowing it.

On Consciousness and Wonder

The hard problem, and what it means that there is one

December 3, 2024·11 min read

David Chalmers distinguished the easy problems of consciousness — explaining memory, attention, the integration of information — from the hard problem: explaining why any of this physical processing is accompanied by subjective experience. The easy problems, he noted, are not actually easy; they will occupy neuroscientists for centuries. But they are tractable in principle: they are problems of mechanism. The hard problem is different in kind. It asks why the mechanism is lit from within. Why should there be anything it is like to be a brain processing information? Why is there experience at all?

What makes this question genuinely hard is that it seems to resist the usual strategies of scientific explanation. When we explain something scientifically, we show how it is produced by or identical to something more fundamental. But experience seems to have no more fundamental level. The redness of red, the pain of pain, the particular quality of this afternoon's light through this window — these are not reducible to neural correlates without a remainder. Something is always left over: the feel of things.

What makes this question genuinely hard is that it seems to resist the usual strategies of scientific explanation.

I am not certain that Chalmers' conclusion — that consciousness cannot be explained in purely physical terms — is correct. Philosophers smarter than I am have argued on both sides of this question for decades. What I am certain of is that the question itself is important, and that the certainty with which some scientists dismiss it is one of the least becoming things about contemporary scientific culture. The person who says consciousness is just computation has not dissolved the mystery; they have waved at it from a distance.

What the hard problem gives us, whatever its ultimate resolution, is a doorway back into wonder. In a culture that has largely domesticated the natural world — explained it, mapped it, numbered it — consciousness remains stubbornly wild. The most intimate thing in the universe turns out to be the thing we understand least. There is something appropriate in this: that the knowing should itself be the last unknown.

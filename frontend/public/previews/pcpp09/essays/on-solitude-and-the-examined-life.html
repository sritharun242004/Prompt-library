On Solitude and the Examined Life

Why the interior life needs room to breathe

June 30, 2024·7 min read

Blaise Pascal observed that all of humanity's problems stem from the inability to sit quietly in a room alone. This is a slight exaggeration, but only slight. What we do when we cannot be alone is reach for stimulation — for noise, for company, for the endless scroll that gives the impression of content while supplying mainly sensation. The avoidance of solitude is not, in most cases, a preference for company; it is a flight from the self that company — and failing that, noise — makes possible.

What Montaigne discovered in his tower was not solitude in the sense of deprivation but solitude in the sense of possession — the experience of having himself to talk to. His essays are records of these conversations: the mind encountering itself on the page, turning ideas over, changing position, doubting and reasserting and doubting again. This is not the solitude of the hermit who has renounced the world, but the solitude of the person who has learned that some of the most interesting company available is one's own.

The examined life requires solitude not because thought is impossible in company — conversation can be a form of examination, as Socrates demonstrated — but because the examination that matters most is the kind that goes on without an audience. We perform for others; we cannot easily help it. The presence of a listener changes what we say and what we think. In solitude, the performance collapses, and what remains is what is actually true. This is sometimes uncomfortable. It is also the condition of genuine self-knowledge.

The capacity for solitude is not given but developed. Children learn it gradually, if they are fortunate enough to have space. Adults must practice it deliberately, against the pull of a culture that offers connectivity as a substitute for depth. The reward for this practice is not loneliness but its opposite: the experience of being genuinely at home in oneself, which makes all genuine company — the company of books, of friends, of landscapes — richer rather than poorer.

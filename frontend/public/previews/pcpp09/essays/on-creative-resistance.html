On Creative Resistance

The shadow that follows every genuine act of making

December 28, 2024·7 min read

Steven Pressfield calls it Resistance (always capitalized, always personal): the force that prevents the writer from writing, the painter from painting, the composer from composing. Its disguises are many — procrastination, perfectionism, distraction, the urgent but less important task that presents itself at the exact moment when the important task has become possible. Resistance is not random. It is, Pressfield argues, precisely proportional to the importance of the work. The more the work matters, the stronger the resistance. This is why trivial work requires no resistance: nothing opposes what does not matter.

Glenn Gould, preparing to record the Goldberg Variations for the second time in 1981, reportedly spent months in a state of paralytic uncertainty — this from one of the most technically gifted pianists who ever lived, returning to music he had recorded at twenty-two and knew better than almost anything else. The resistance Gould faced was not incompetence but its opposite: the knowledge of how profound the music is, and the corresponding weight of the responsibility to do it justice. The beginner does not feel this. They have not yet understood what is at stake.

What makes resistance particularly devious is that it speaks in the voice of wisdom. It does not say: I am resistance, and I am preventing you from doing your work. It says: This is not quite ready. You need more time. You should think about this more. The conditions are not right. Some of this may be true — not all hesitation is resistance, and some postponements are genuinely necessary. But resistance knows this and exploits it, borrowing the language of discernment to excuse procrastination.

The only reliable antidote to resistance is sitting down and beginning. Not the conditions for beginning, not the idea of beginning, but the actual physical act: paper, pen, hand moving. Or keyboard, fingers pressing. Or instrument, bow touching string. The first notes do not have to be good. They have to exist. Resistance cannot survive the actual commencement of work; it lives only in the space before the beginning. Once the work has started, a different dynamic takes over — the work begins to teach, and the teacher is always more interesting than the excuse.

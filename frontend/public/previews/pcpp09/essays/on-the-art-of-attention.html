On the Art of Attention

How looking slowly changes what we see

March 10, 2025·9 min read

There is a difference between looking and seeing, between hearing and listening. The first is passive: sensation arrives at the threshold of the mind and is catalogued, filed, forgotten. The second requires a kind of renunciation — a willingness to set aside the self that wants to conclude, to categorize, to move on. When Cézanne spent hours in front of Mont Sainte-Victoire returning to paint it again and again, he was not being inefficient. He was practicing the only discipline that matters to a painter: staying with the thing itself long enough for it to disclose its actual nature.

Simone Weil, writing about prayer, described attention as the capacity to suspend the movement of desire long enough to receive what is actually there. This is, I think, not only a description of spiritual practice but of aesthetic experience at its most refined. The reader who rushes through a poem in search of its meaning has found nothing. The meaning of a poem, if that word applies at all, is discovered only in the act of dwelling with it — reading aloud, pausing, returning. The poem that has been merely decoded has not been read.

What prevents attention is not distraction, exactly, but the anxiety of incompletion. We rush toward resolution because uncertainty is uncomfortable. To look at a painting without knowing what it means feels wasteful to the modern mind, which has been trained to produce outputs. And yet the painting offers its gifts only to those who can accept the temporary vertigo of not-knowing. The great works of art are not puzzles to be solved but presences to be encountered.

I have been trying, lately, to practice the attention that Weil describes in the small transactions of daily life — to look at the particular tree rather than the general idea of trees, to hear what the specific person is saying rather than preparing my response. It is harder than meditation and more immediately rewarding. The world, attended to honestly, turns out to be inexhaustibly strange. The ordinary, given sufficient looking, becomes luminous with particularity.

On Reading Aloud

How the voice reveals what the eye misses

November 20, 2024·6 min read

The practice of reading aloud was once so common as to require no defense. Augustine, writing in the fourth century, described his amazement at watching Ambrose read without moving his lips — silent reading was remarkable enough to be worth recording. For most of human history, texts were heard before they were read, and even private reading was typically voiced. The silent, internal reading that we now consider normal is a relatively recent development, and it has costs that we rarely notice because we have never known anything else.

When you read aloud, you engage muscles that silent reading does not use — the diaphragm, the tongue, the lips, the entire apparatus of breath. These muscles do not merely relay the text; they respond to it, and their responses reveal things that the eye, moving rapidly across a page, misses. The sentence that reads smoothly in silence may stagger when voiced; the one that seemed awkward on the page may turn out to have a rhythm that only the body could hear. Poets know this, which is why so many of them compose by speaking aloud.

There is also an argument from presence. Reading aloud creates a witness relationship between the reader and the text — you are, briefly, the text's advocate, its representative, responsible for its passage from marks on a page to meaning in a room. This responsibility changes how you read. You cannot skim when you are reading aloud. You cannot pass over what you do not understand. The text demands from you a fidelity that silent reading, with its easy illusion of comprehension, does not require.

The simplest argument for reading aloud is also the most powerful: the experience is different, and the difference is valuable. To hear a sentence that you have also read is to encounter it for the first time again — to have it arrive from outside rather than form inside, to feel its weight in air rather than imagine its weight on a page. The great texts of the tradition were composed for this medium. To return them to it is not affectation but fidelity.

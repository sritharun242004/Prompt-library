The Slow Time of Nature

What geology teaches us about patience

September 27, 2024·7 min read

John McPhee coined the phrase "deep time" to describe the geological timescale — the billions of years during which the earth has been folding, erupting, eroding, and rebuilding itself in utter indifference to human schedules. To stand at the bottom of the Grand Canyon and read the rock layers is to understand that you are reading a sentence that takes 1.7 billion years to complete. The canyon walls are a kind of scripture, but written in a language that makes our own seem very young.

The effect of contemplating deep time is, paradoxically, not to make human life feel insignificant but to put urgency in its place. Most of what we consider urgent is urgent only by the standards of the quarterly report or the news cycle. Geological time reminds us that there is another frame in which the frantic pace of contemporary life appears as a brief and rather anxious interlude. Mountains grow at the rate of fingernails. Continents drift. The climate oscillates. None of this can be hurried.

The effect of contemplating deep time is, paradoxically, not to make human life feel insignificant but to put urgency in its place.

There is a tradition in Western thought, running from Pascal through modern existentialism, of finding nature's scale terrifying — of experiencing the silence of infinite spaces as a threat. But this tradition misses something. Nature's scale is not threatening; it is clarifying. It does not diminish us so much as it correctly calibrates us. To know that this hillside has been here for forty million years is not to feel irrelevant but to feel, briefly, at the right size.

The naturalist and poet Gary Snyder wrote that the practice of sitting quietly and attending to what is actually here is itself a form of geological literacy — a learning to read the time embedded in the land. The moss on the north face of the boulder, the rings in the fallen tree, the meander of the river that will, in ten thousand years, have cut its way a mile to the west: these are texts in the language of deep time. To read them is to be educated in a patience that is not resignation but attentiveness.

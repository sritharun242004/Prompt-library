A Field Guide to Wonder

How to recover the capacity that childhood gave us freely

February 28, 2025·9 min read

The philosopher Mary-Jane Rubenstein distinguishes between wonder and curiosity in a way that has stayed with me. Curiosity, she writes, wants to consume its object — it wants to know, and once it knows, it moves on. Wonder, by contrast, is arrested by its object; it stands before the thing and refuses the forward movement of inquiry. The curious person asks what it is. The wondering person asks that it is at all. Wonder is not the engine of inquiry but its prior condition: it is the experience of being stopped by the sheer fact of existence.

Children are naturally in this state because they have not yet learned to take the ordinary for granted. The falling leaf, the shadow on the wall, the repeating pattern in the kitchen tile — these are not boring to the child because they have not yet been catalogued and filed as belonging to the category "things that do not require attention." Adults have performed this filing so thoroughly that we must actively work to undo it. We have to teach ourselves to see the ordinary as extraordinary again, which is difficult precisely because the ordinary has become a habit.

I have found that music is one of the most reliable routes back to wonder. Not just any music, and not as background — but music attended to, in silence, without doing anything else. The experience of listening to Schubert's final piano sonata as though hearing it for the first time, even after hundreds of hearings, requires something like the active practice of unknowing: a deliberate setting aside of familiarity in order to hear what is actually there. It is harder than it sounds. But when it works, the music opens into something vast and indefinite, and for a few minutes the world is restored to its original strangeness.

The capacity for wonder can be practiced. Attention is its instrument; slowness is its method; willingness to be surprised is its only prerequisite. The world, attended to honestly, is inexhaustibly strange. Familiarity is not a property of things but a property of the attention we bring to them. The ordinary object, looked at long enough, always turns out to be extraordinary — to be precisely the kind of thing that should not exist, and yet does. This discovery, repeated daily, is what wonder consists in. It is available to everyone, and it costs nothing but time.

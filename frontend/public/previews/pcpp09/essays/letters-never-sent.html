Letters Never Sent

On the diary, the unsent letter, and the truth that requires no witness

August 5, 2024·7 min read

Virginia Woolf's diary was not a polished literary performance. It was, as she described it, a room of her own inside language — a place to think without the pressure of an audience. The entries range from brilliant to petty, from visionary to trivially complaining. Their value is not despite this range but because of it: the diary's refusal to self-edit is its deepest form of honesty. What survives to us is not the version of Woolf that wanted to be seen but the version of Woolf that actually was.

The unsent letter — written in rage or grief or love too intense to survive the transit to another person — is similarly liberated. When Franz Kafka wrote letters to his father that he never delivered, he was not failing at communication; he was succeeding at something the delivered letter cannot do. The delivered letter is always written in awareness of its recipient, always shaped by the anticipated response, always partly a performance. The unsent letter is addressed to the truth.

The unsent letter — written in rage or grief or love too intense to survive the transit to another person — is similarly liberated.

There is a craft to private writing that is different from the craft of public writing, though the two are related. Public writing must meet the reader where they are, must explain, contextualize, persuade. Private writing can omit all this — it can presuppose the whole of the writer's inner life, can refer to matters that need no introduction because the writer and the audience are the same person. This is what makes the diary so strange to read in retrospect: the clipped references, the assumed context, the shorthand of a self talking to itself.

I have kept a journal, intermittently, for twenty years. Reading back through it is like reading letters from a stranger who happens to share my name. The concerns are not mine; the preoccupations have resolved or deepened beyond recognition; the handwriting has changed. But there is a fidelity in those pages that I cannot achieve in public writing — a record of what it actually felt like, at a particular moment, to be me. This seems worth something, even if I am not sure what.

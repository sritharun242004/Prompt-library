The Hidden Life of Numbers

On the unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics

July 12, 2024·9 min read

In 1960, the physicist Eugene Wigner published an essay titled "The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics in the Natural Sciences." The title captures the central mystery: why should abstract structures developed by mathematicians for purely aesthetic reasons — group theory, complex analysis, non-Euclidean geometry — turn out, decades or centuries later, to be precisely the structures needed to describe physical reality? The mathematician is not trying to describe nature; they are following the logic of number. And yet nature, it turns out, was using the same logic all along.

The Pythagoreans believed that the universe was ultimately made of numbers — that number was the arche, the underlying stuff, in the way that later philosophers thought it was water or fire or atoms. This position has generally been treated as a charming proto-scientific error. But the more physics advances, the more it looks like the Pythagoreans were not entirely wrong. What is a particle but a solution to a system of differential equations? What is spacetime but a geometric manifold? At the deepest levels of physical description, the world becomes indistinguishable from mathematics.

The Pythagoreans believed that the universe was ultimately made of numbers — that number was the arche, the underlying stuff, in the way that later philosophers thought it was water or fire or atoms.

This is not merely a fact about physics. It suggests something about the relationship between structure and reality that we have barely begun to think through. If the world is mathematical in its deep nature — if the laws of nature are not merely described by mathematics but constituted by it — then the mathematician's aesthetic intuitions about what is beautiful, what is elegant, what is surprisingly simple, are not arbitrary. They are tracking something real about the structure of what exists.

There is a kind of wonder available in mathematics that is different from the wonder of natural beauty or human art — more austere, more demanding, and in its way more profound. To follow a proof through to its conclusion and feel the satisfaction of the Q.E.D. is to have touched something that was true before anyone was there to know it. The theorem does not become true when we prove it. We prove it because it was already true. This is not a small thing to feel.

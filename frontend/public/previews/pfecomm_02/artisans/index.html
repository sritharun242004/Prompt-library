The Makers

Artisans & Makers

Every piece we sell was made by a craftsperson with a name, a place, and a lineage. These are some of the artisans whose hands are in everything we carry.

Hand Block Printing

Ramji Khatri

Bagru, Rajasthan

Ramji is a fifth-generation block printer from Bagru, where the practice of natural-dye block printing stretches back centuries. He carves his own teak blocks, a skill his grandfather passed down with the instruction that every pattern must contain an intentional imperfection — so the maker is never confused with a machine.

Kantha Embroidery

Annapurna Devi

Murshidabad, West Bengal

Annapurna leads a cooperative of 34 women in Murshidabad who practise the kantha tradition — the art of stitching old saris into new cloth with a running stitch that tells stories from daily life. Each piece in the cooperative carries a shared identity and an individual hand.

Jamdani Weaving

Hussain Ansari

Fulia, West Bengal

Hussain weaves on a pit loom that his father built in 1981. Jamdani — the supplementary weft technique that creates its distinctive floating motifs — requires full visual concentration and cannot be mechanised. A single saree takes Hussain three weeks. Each one is GI tagged and carries his weaver certificate number.

Natural Dye

Savitri Meghwal

Jodhpur, Rajasthan

Savitri runs a natural dye workshop in Jodhpur using indigo, pomegranate rind, henna, and iron mud. She learned the craft from her mother, who learned it from hers, in an unbroken line that predates synthetic dyes by generations. Every colour she produces is seasonal — the marigold yield in October reads differently to the one in March.

Ajrakh Block Printing

Gopaldas Prajapati

Kutch, Gujarat

Ajrakh is one of the most complex resist-printing traditions in the world — up to sixteen separate steps for a single cloth. Gopaldas trained for eight years before he was permitted to touch a printing block. He now leads a workshop of twelve craftspeople in Kutch and has recently revived two extinct block patterns found in a 1940s archive photograph.

Chikankari Embroidery

Meenakshi Bai

Lucknow, Uttar Pradesh

Meenakshi is a master of the shadow-work technique within chikankari, where stitches on the reverse of the cloth create a subtle pattern visible from the front. She works from home and has trained her daughters and two nieces — ensuring the most refined stitches in her community will continue into the next generation.

Pochampally Ikat

Yellaiah Padmashali

Pochampally, Telangana

Yellaiah weaves double ikat — one of only a handful of places in the world where both warp and weft are resist-dyed before weaving, requiring the pattern to be planned in three dimensions before a single thread is placed on the loom. His geometric designs are registered under the Pochampally GI tag.

Zari & Zardozi

Fatima Siddiqui

Varanasi, Uttar Pradesh

Fatima embroiders with real silver and gold wire — a technique that Mughal courts elevated to high art and that Varanasi has kept alive through economic difficulty and changing fashion. She works under a magnifying glass and has been known to spend forty hours on a single motif the size of a thumb.

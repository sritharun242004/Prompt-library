Switching Into Tech: What No One Tells You

I transitioned from mechanical engineering to software development at 28. Three years later I am a senior engineer. Here is what I wish someone had told me at the beginning.

January 30, 20257 min read

When I decided to switch careers at 28, I looked for advice and found two kinds: "It is too late, don't bother" from cynics, and "Anyone can learn to code in 12 weeks!" from bootcamp marketing material. Neither was helpful.

The truth is more nuanced and more encouraging than either extreme. Career switching into software development is genuinely possible, but the path is rarely linear and the things that matter are not the ones you expect.

What Actually Transfers

The sceptics are wrong that non-technical backgrounds are useless. A mechanical engineering background gave me something that took some of my bootcamp-grad colleagues years to develop: a habit of thinking about systems before thinking about implementations.

When a product manager describes a feature, I am thinking about data flow and failure modes before I am thinking about component hierarchy. When debugging, I form a hypothesis and test it before reaching for console.log everywhere. These habits come from years of solving physical problems, and they transfer almost perfectly.

Whatever your previous field — teaching, finance, science, trade work — you have domain knowledge and problem-solving patterns that will make you a better engineer, not despite your non-traditional path, but because of it.

The First Six Months Are the Hardest

Nobody warns you about the disorientation of being a beginner again. You go from being competent — perhaps excellent — in your field to not understanding error messages. The impostor syndrome is intense.

What helped me: treating the confusion as data rather than evidence of inadequacy. Confusion means you are encountering something new. That is learning. The engineers I respect most are comfortable saying "I don't know — let me find out." They became comfortable with that by spending a lot of time not knowing.

Be patient with the gap between your general intelligence and your current technical knowledge. The gap closes faster than you think, but it does not close overnight.

Build Things No One Else Will Build

The biggest mistake career-switchers make is building the same portfolio projects as everyone else. Todo apps. Weather apps. Netflix clones. These signal that you can follow a tutorial; they do not signal that you can solve a problem.

Build something that only you would build. I built a data visualisation tool for failure mode analysis — a problem I understood from mechanical engineering — in my first three months of learning web development. It was ugly and poorly architected, but it showed domain knowledge and initiative that generic portfolio projects cannot.

Get Into a Real Codebase as Fast as Possible

Tutorials teach you syntax. Real codebases teach you programming. The jump from tutorial to production code is enormous and necessary.

Contributing to open source, even in small ways — fixing typos in documentation, writing a failing test, adding a missing type definition — exposes you to professional conventions and review processes that accelerate your learning faster than any course.

On Bootcamps and Degrees

Bootcamps work for some people and do not work for others. The quality varies enormously. The alumni network and hiring partnerships matter more than the curriculum. Research both before committing.

A CS degree is still the highest-signal credential in the industry, but it is not required. Strong portfolio projects, genuine contributions to open source, and a demonstrated ability to learn are increasingly sufficient — especially in Singapore's tech market, where the supply of self-taught developers with domain expertise is undervalued.

The Long Game

Three years into my career switch, I am a senior engineer. I still learn something every day. The engineers I work with who started conventionally are not ahead of me — some are, some are not. What matters now is curiosity, consistency, and the willingness to go deep on hard problems.

The path is available to you. It requires more work than a boot camp brochure suggests and less time than a cynic claims. Start building. Start slowly. Stay curious.

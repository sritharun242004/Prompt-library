Framework

Smiling Curve

In any value chain, the stages at both extremes — components/IP at one end and distribution/brand at the other — capture more profit than the assembly and manufacturing stages in the middle.

The Smiling Curve was first described by Stan Shih, founder of Acer, in the early 1990s to explain why Taiwanese PC assemblers were being squeezed despite operating in a growing industry.

The insight is geometric: plot value-added against the stages of a value chain (from raw materials/components through assembly to distribution and marketing), and the result is a U-shape — high at both ends, low in the middle.

In electronics, this meant chip designers (Intel, Qualcomm) and brand/retailers (Apple, Best Buy) captured margin while assemblers (Foxconn, Wistron) competed on thin margins for volume business.

The curve has since been observed in pharmaceuticals (molecule IP and distribution vs manufacturing), automotive (design/engineering and dealer networks vs assembly), and media (IP creation and audience relationships vs production/printing).

In AI, the curve is emerging clearly: semiconductor IP (NVIDIA, TSMC) and distribution platforms (Apple, Microsoft, Google) capture margin, while model training and serving — the middle of the AI value chain — faces increasing commoditisation from open-source alternatives and competition.

Strategic implication: businesses in the middle of any value chain face structural margin pressure. The response is either to move toward an extreme (acquire IP or distribution) or to become so operationally excellent that scale compensates for thin margins.

Applied Examples

  • NVIDIA capturing AI chip margin while model labs compete intensely
  • Apple capturing distribution margin on every App Store transaction
  • Pharmaceutical companies earning returns on molecule IP, not manufacturing
  • Music labels owning catalogue IP while streaming platforms commoditise distribution

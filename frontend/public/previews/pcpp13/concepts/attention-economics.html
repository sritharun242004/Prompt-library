Attention Economics

In an information-abundant environment, the scarce resource is not information but human attention. Businesses compete for a finite cognitive budget — and the economics of that competition are as rigorous as any commodity market.

Herbert Simon first formalised the attention economy concept in 1971: "a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention." The insight predated the internet; the internet made it commercially exploitable at scale.

The economics of attention follow several distinctive rules:

Attention is finite and non-renewable. A human has approximately 16 waking hours per day. Every minute spent on one application is a minute unavailable to another. This makes attention zero-sum at the individual level, even when it feels abundant at the aggregate level.

Attention is unequal across contexts. Attention allocated to a purchase decision is more valuable than attention allocated to passive entertainment. Advertisers have always known this — but the commoditisation of passive attention (display advertising) relative to intent-driven attention (search advertising) is a direct expression of this inequality.

Platform design is attention engineering. Variable reward schedules (notifications, infinite scroll, algorithmic feeds) are engineering choices designed to maximise time-on-platform. The techniques were borrowed from behavioural psychology and slot machine design, and they work.

The backlash creates new opportunities. Awareness of attention engineering has created a market for anti-attention-engineering products: newsletters (time-shifted, low-frequency), podcasts (passive), long-form analysis (depth over volume), Kindle (no notifications). The premium on sustained, uninterrupted engagement is rising.

The AI attention question: If AI models can synthesise and deliver information more efficiently, the attention required to stay informed may decrease. This could increase the value of the remaining attention time — or redistribute it toward new forms of engagement. The direction is not yet clear.

Applied Examples

  • TikTok winning share from Facebook by optimising for passive engagement
  • Podcast growth driven by multitasking-compatible audio format
  • Email newsletters winning back readers fleeing algorithmic feeds
  • Netflix losing sleep to gain viewing hours

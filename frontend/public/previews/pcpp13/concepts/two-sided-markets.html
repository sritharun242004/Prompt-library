Two-Sided Markets

Platforms serving two distinct user groups create value by enabling interactions between them. Cross-side network effects differ structurally from same-side effects and require different pricing and growth strategies.

Two-sided market theory formalises the economics of platforms that serve two distinct user groups whose value from the platform depends on the size of the other group.

The classic formulation distinguishes between same-side effects (more users of your type makes the platform more valuable for you — as with social networks) and cross-side effects (more users of the opposite type makes the platform more valuable for you — as with buyers and sellers on a marketplace).

The pricing puzzle is the central practical insight: in a two-sided market, you cannot set prices on each side independently. Subsidising one side (typically the harder-to-attract side) to grow it increases the value for the other side, from which you can then extract more revenue.

Credit card networks are the canonical case: issuers pay cardholders rewards; merchants pay interchange fees. The cardholder subsidy is economically rational because it grows cardholder adoption, which makes the card more valuable to merchants, who pay the fees that fund the subsidy plus profit.

The multi-homing question determines the platform's leverage. If one side of the market multi-homes freely (operates on multiple platforms), that side's bargaining power increases and the platform's ability to extract margin decreases.

The chicken-and-egg problem is the founding challenge: neither side of a two-sided market has value without the other. How do you attract buyers without sellers, or sellers without buyers? Successful solutions typically involve subsidising one side heavily at launch (often the demand side) or creating artificial supply (as OkCupid did by creating fake profiles, as Airbnb did by photographing early listings professionally).

Applied Examples

  • Uber subsidised rider acquisition to grow supply of drivers
  • iOS priced developer tools cheaply to grow app ecosystem for users
  • LinkedIn offers free profiles to attract employers who pay for recruitment
  • Newspapers historically subsidised reader price via advertiser revenue

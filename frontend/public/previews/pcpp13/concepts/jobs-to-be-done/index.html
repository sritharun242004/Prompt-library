Jobs to be Done

Customers do not buy products; they hire them to do a job. Understanding the functional, emotional, and social dimensions of that job reveals why competitors that look dissimilar are actually fighting for the same hire.

Jobs to be Done (JTBD) theory, developed by Clayton Christensen and colleagues, offers a different unit of analysis for competitive dynamics. Instead of asking "who are our competitors?" or "what features do customers want?", JTBD asks: "What job is the customer hiring this product to do?"

The canonical example is the milkshake: McDonald's discovered that early-morning milkshake purchasers were not competing with other milkshakes — they were hiring the milkshake to do the job of making a long morning commute less boring. The real competitors were bagels, bananas, and coffee.

This reframing has three practical implications:

1. The job, not the product, defines the market. Netflix competes with sleep (as Reed Hastings famously noted) — not just with other streaming services. The job is "entertain me when I have discretionary time"; sleep is the alternative that wins when Netflix fails to be engaging enough.

2. Jobs have functional, emotional, and social dimensions. Enterprise software is hired to do a functional job (process invoices, manage inventory) but also an emotional job (reduce anxiety about compliance) and a social job (signal that the organisation is professionally managed). Pricing and positioning that addresses only the functional dimension underprices the full value being delivered.

3. The job is stable; the product is not. The job of "staying informed about what matters in my professional field" has existed for decades. The products hired to do it have changed — physical newspapers, magazines, RSS readers, newsletters, podcasts. Understanding the job predicts which new products will compete and which incumbents are vulnerable.

Applied Examples

  • Slack hired to reduce email anxiety, not just improve communication
  • iPhone hired for identity signalling as much as smartphone functionality
  • Substack hired to give writers autonomy from platform dependency
  • Notion hired for the job of "feel organised and in control"

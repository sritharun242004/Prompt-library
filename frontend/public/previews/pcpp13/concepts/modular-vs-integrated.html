Modular vs Integrated

At any given moment in a technology market, the architecture that wins — modular (open, standardised interfaces) or integrated (proprietary, end-to-end control) — depends on where the performance bottleneck in the market sits.

The modular versus integrated framework, developed most rigorously by Clayton Christensen, holds that the optimal architecture for a technology business depends on the maturity of the market and the location of the performance bottleneck.

When performance is not good enough (the product cannot yet satisfy the most demanding customers), integrated architectures win. Integration allows optimisation across all interfaces in the system — removing the coordination overhead of modular interfaces and squeezing out every increment of performance. Early mainframes, early automobiles, and early smartphones were all integrated for this reason.

When performance is more than good enough (even the lowest-cost product satisfies most customers), modular architectures win. Once performance exceeds customer needs, the basis of competition shifts to price, convenience, and flexibility — which modular designs can provide at lower cost by specialising each component.

The practical application is identifying where a market sits in this cycle. The smartphone market in 2007 was clearly performance-not-good-enough: Apple's integrated approach (controlling hardware, software, and services) produced a product that outperformed every modular competitor. The PC market in the 1990s was clearly good-enough: Intel + Microsoft's modular architecture allowed Dell and Compaq to compete purely on price.

The AI market in 2026 is in an interesting transitional state. Foundation model training is still performance-not-good-enough for the most demanding applications — which favours integrated approaches. Application development is good-enough — which favours modular, API-based approaches. The architecture that wins at each layer of the stack should differ accordingly.

Applied Examples

  • Apple winning smartphones with integration when Android was not good enough
  • AWS winning cloud infrastructure with modular services once virtualisation matured
  • Tesla transitioning from integrated to more modular as EV performance matured
  • NVIDIA winning AI training with integrated GPU+software stack

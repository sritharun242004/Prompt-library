Aggregation Theory

Platforms that aggregate demand and own the user relationship can commoditise their suppliers and extract the majority of industry value, regardless of who produces the underlying goods or services.

Aggregation Theory, first articulated systematically by Ben Thompson, holds that the internet fundamentally changed the economics of distribution. In the pre-internet era, distribution was scarce and valuable — owning a broadcast tower, a printing press, or a retail shelf gave you power over everyone who wanted to reach consumers.

The internet made distribution effectively free. This inverted the power structure: the scarce resource became user relationships and user attention, not the ability to distribute content or goods.

Businesses that figured this out — Google, Facebook, Amazon, Uber — did not primarily produce anything. They aggregated users at scale, then used that aggregated demand to commoditise suppliers. Publishers became dependent on Google for traffic; drivers became commoditised by Uber; merchants became dependent on Amazon for discovery.

The theory predicts that any platform sitting between suppliers and consumers, once it achieves sufficient scale on both sides, can extract the majority of the economic surplus in its market.

Three conditions are required for full aggregation power: (1) the platform must own the user relationship directly; (2) suppliers must need the platform more than the platform needs any individual supplier; (3) the platform must be the primary interface through which users discover and access the supply.

Applied Examples

  • Google commoditised news publishers by aggregating search intent
  • Uber commoditised taxi drivers by aggregating ride demand
  • Amazon commoditised merchants by aggregating purchase intent
  • Netflix commoditised mid-tier studios by aggregating streaming subscribers

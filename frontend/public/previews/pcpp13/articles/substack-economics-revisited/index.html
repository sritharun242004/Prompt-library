Substack Economics Revisited

Substack's model is now mature enough to evaluate with real data. What the numbers show about which writers succeed, which fail, and what Substack's own economics look like challenges several popular assumptions.

Big Tech

Substack launched in 2017 with a simple model: writers keep 90% of subscription revenue; Substack takes 10%. The model has scaled to millions of subscribers and hundreds of thousands of writers. The data are now available to assess what actually works.

The winner's dilemma. The top 10 Substack publications account for a disproportionate share of total Substack revenue. This is not an anomaly; it is a structural feature of any marketplace with power law distributions. The platform grows by adding volume at the long tail, but monetises primarily through a small number of dominant publications.

The conversion ceiling. Across the Substack ecosystem, median free-to-paid conversion for publications in general interest categories (culture, politics, lifestyle) is approximately 2%. For specialist professional and B2B publications, it rises to 5-8%. The conversion rate is the single most important variable and it is almost entirely determined by content differentiation, not marketing.

What Substack gets. A publication with 50,000 free subscribers converting at 5% to a $10/month subscription generates $25,000/month gross revenue. Substack's 10% take is $2,500/month from that one publication. At tens of thousands of publications on the platform, the aggregate is substantial — but only if the top tier of publications stays.

The strategic risk for Substack is that large publications, generating enough revenue to justify the overhead, will migrate to self-hosted or Ghost-based setups to recapture the 10% fee. Substack's moat is its discovery and social features — but these are replicable.

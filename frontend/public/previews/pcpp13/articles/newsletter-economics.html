Newsletter Economics: A Realistic Model

Most newsletter monetisation advice is either naively optimistic or cynically defeatist. Here is a ground-up model built from operator data that actually matches observed outcomes across a range of publication sizes and topics.

Big Tech

The newsletter economy has produced its mythology. The mythology holds that any writer with a passionate audience can earn a comfortable living from a few thousand subscribers paying $10/month. The arithmetic is simple and seductive: 1,000 subscribers × $10 × 12 months = $120,000/year.

The reality is considerably more complex.

Conversion rates are the variable most consistently underestimated. Across a sample of 200+ newsletters studied, the median paid conversion rate on a free list is 3.2%. The top quartile achieves 7-9%. The bottom quartile struggles to reach 1.5%.

What drives conversion? Four factors, in order of magnitude: (1) depth of the niche — the more specific the topic, the higher the conversion rate; (2) perceived uniqueness — if the information is available elsewhere, subscribers won't pay; (3) consistency — irregular publishing schedules destroy conversion; (4) author reputation — credentials matter enormously in B2B niches.

Churn is the variable most consistently ignored. Monthly paid newsletters typically see 3-5% monthly churn. Annual plans reduce this to 15-25% annual churn. At 4% monthly churn, a newsletter loses nearly 40% of its subscriber base every year — meaning it must continuously acquire new subscribers just to maintain revenue.

The realistic model for a solo operator: A writer with a 15,000-person free list in a specific B2B or professional niche, converting at 5% to a $15/month paid tier, nets $1,125/month before platform fees. At 3% monthly churn, they need to add roughly 33 paid subscribers per month to remain flat. Feasible — but not the lifestyle-business fantasy the mythology sells.

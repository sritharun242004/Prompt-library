Aggregation Theory Revisited

Ben Thompson's Aggregation Theory remains the most useful single lens for understanding platform power. A decade of evidence has exposed its limits — and revealed exactly where the theory needs to grow. This is a reckoning.

Aggregation TheoryBig Tech

Aggregation Theory, first articulated by Ben Thompson in 2015, holds that the internet inverted the fundamental economics of media distribution. Before the internet, distribution was scarce and valuable; after it, distribution became free, and the scarce resource became user attention and relationships.

The theory predicts that any business sitting between suppliers and consumers — aggregating both at scale — can commoditise its suppliers and extract the majority of industry value, regardless of who produces the underlying goods or services. Google commoditised publishers. Facebook commoditised friends. Uber commoditised drivers.

A decade on, the evidence is largely supportive. But three structural anomalies demand explanation.

The Hardware Exception

Apple does not fit the aggregation model cleanly. Apple owns hardware, software, and the marketplace atop it — a vertically integrated stack that should, in theory, be vulnerable to an aggregator that separates these layers. Android tried. It partially succeeded. Yet Apple's Services revenue has grown to compound at rates that suggest the Aggregation Theory's predictions do not hold when the platform controls the device.

The explanation lies in what Thompson later called the "iPhone bundling": when the device itself becomes the relationship, the supplier-aggregator-consumer hierarchy collapses. Apple is simultaneously all three layers, which conventional Aggregation Theory cannot model cleanly.

The Regulatory Inflection

Aggregation Theory's power derives partly from the absence of countervailing force. As platforms grew dominant, regulators in the EU and, increasingly, the US began imposing structural obligations — interoperability mandates, third-party marketplace access, fee ceilings — that artificially reduce the aggregator's leverage over suppliers.

The theory must be updated to model regulatory risk as a first-class variable, not an external shock.

The AI Disruption

The emergence of generative AI introduces a new question: what happens to an aggregator when the cost of producing the supply it aggregates falls to near-zero? If anyone can generate competitive content, the uniqueness of any supplier's contribution to the aggregator's catalogue approaches zero — which should strengthen the aggregator. But if AI also enables new direct-to-consumer channels, the aggregator's hold on user relationships weakens simultaneously.

This is the central strategic tension of the current era: AI commoditises supply and potentially disintermediates distribution at the same time.

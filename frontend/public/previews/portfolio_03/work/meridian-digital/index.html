Challenge

Meridian's existing patient portal had a 23% task-completion rate for appointment scheduling. Patients were calling the front desk to do things the portal was supposed to handle — costing the network an estimated £1.2M annually in administrative overhead. Three previous redesign attempts had failed because they were led by internal IT teams optimising for database architecture rather than patient behaviour. The clinical leadership were under regulatory pressure to demonstrate digital accessibility compliance by Q4. The brief required us to work within the existing backend infrastructure with no new data architecture.

Approach

We conducted 28 user sessions across three patient demographics — elderly patients, parents booking for children, and working-age adults with complex appointment histories. Every assumption the internal team held was wrong. Elderly patients wanted large tap targets and minimal steps — not simplified language. Parents needed multi-profile management, not a simpler single-patient view. The solution was structural, not cosmetic: we rebuilt the navigation architecture from a department-led hierarchy to a task-led one, and reduced the scheduling flow from eleven steps to three.

Outcome

Task completion rate increased to 78% within the first month of deployment. Call volume for scheduling dropped 41% — recovering £490K in administrative costs in year one. NPS went from 22 to 67. The portal passed WCAG 2.1 AA compliance audit with zero critical failures — a first for the network. Meridian's CTO presented the project at the NHS Digital Summit as a case study in patient-centred design.

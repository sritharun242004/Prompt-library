On Building in Public

The accountability structures that actually work

By Mara Ellison·Friday, May 3, 2024·6 min read

I have been building in public for two years now — sharing revenue numbers, posting failure postmortems, narrating decisions as I make them. The number I hear most from people considering doing the same thing is about audience growth. They want to know how many followers they will gain. This is the wrong question. The right question is: what kind of accountability do you want to create for yourself?

Accountability is the real product of building in public. When you commit to showing your numbers, you stop rounding up in your own head. When you post about what you are working on, you become accountable to a version of yourself that exists in public, and that version is harder to quietly abandon than the one living only in your notes app. The audience is almost incidental — what matters is the external record.

The darker side of this, which is less often written about, is that public accountability creates perverse incentives if you are not careful. The pressure to show progress can make you optimize for visible metrics over meaningful ones. The temptation to curate the narrative — to make the failures legible and the wins more dramatic — is constant. Building in public honestly requires a daily practice of resisting the story you want to tell about yourself in favor of the one that is actually true.

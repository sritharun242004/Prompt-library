Our Boutiques

Five addresses. Five cities. Each boutique designed as an extension of the archive — not a store, but a curated environment. All visits by appointment.

Mumbai

INDIA

Mumbai

Kala Ghoda

Our oldest boutique, established in 2004, occupies a heritage building in the cultural heart of Bombay. The interior — designed by Shimul Jhaveri — is an exercise in compressed grandeur: raw stone walls, hand-knotted cotton dhurries, and display cases of aged teak that have held more couture garments than most museum archives.

Delhi

INDIA

Delhi

Mehrauli

In the shadow of the Qutub Minar, our Delhi atelier occupies a converted haveli with a courtyard garden where clients sometimes wait in contemplative silence before their appointments. The boutique's inner room — the private atelier — is accessible only by invitation and houses the archive: past collections, textile samples, and the private client records of over two decades.

Kolkata

INDIA

Kolkata

Park Street

Our Calcutta boutique is our spiritual home — the city where the house was founded and where our earliest collaborations with kantha and jamdani weavers began. The Park Street address, in a 1930s colonial mansion, maintains an atmosphere of old-city intellectual elegance: high ceilings, terrazzo floors, and shelves of books on Indian craft and textile history that clients are encouraged to read.

Hyderabad

INDIA

Hyderabad

Banjara Hills

The Hyderabad boutique opened in 2016, in a city with a jewellery and textile culture as deep as any in the subcontinent. The Nizam's court invented standards of luxury that contemporary India has yet to surpass. Our Banjara Hills atelier pays homage to that legacy with Bidriwork panels on the walls and a jewellery salon that operates by separate appointment.

London

UNITED KINGDOM

London

Mayfair

Our London address — in a Georgian townhouse on Mount Street, Mayfair — is the house's only international boutique and its most architecturally ambitious space. The interior was designed in collaboration with London architect John Pawson: minimal, monastic, and in deliberate contrast to the ornamental richness of the garments within. The effect is that of a museum that has agreed, reluctantly, to let you try things on.

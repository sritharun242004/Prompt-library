Chirashi Sushi Bowl

1h total
Pro Chef
Serves 2
4.9
Chef Marcus Thorne

Scattered sushi: a bed of seasoned shari rice crowned with seasonal fish, pickled vegetables, and edible flowers — the Japanese art of beautiful impermanence on a plate. Each component is individually perfect; together, they are extraordinary.

Prep

40 min

Cook

20 min

Total

1h

Mise en Place

120 g Sushi-grade salmon

thinly sliced

120 g Sushi-grade tuna (akami)

thinly sliced

80 g Sushi-grade yellowtail

thinly sliced

300 g Japanese short-grain rice

3 tbsp Rice wine vinegar

1 tbsp Sugar

1 tsp Fine sea salt

1 portion Tamago (Japanese rolled omelette)

sliced into strips

1/2 small Cucumber

julienned

2 tbsp Tobiko (flying fish roe)

20 g Pickled ginger

1 sheet Nori

cut into thin strips

Method

  1. 1

    Cook the shari rice

    Cook rice with a strip of kombu. While hot, season with vinegar, sugar, and salt mixture. Fold gently with a wooden paddle — do not stir. Fan while folding to achieve the glossy surface.

    Chef's Note

    Shari rice should be body temperature when served — never cold from the refrigerator.

    30 min

  2. 2

    Prepare the toppings

    Slice fish against the grain at a 45° angle for maximum surface area and tenderness. Prepare all other elements before assembling.

    15 min

  3. 3

    Assemble the bowl

    Divide rice between two bowls. Arrange fish slices in a fan or scatter pattern — chirashi means "scattered." Add tamago strips, cucumber, tobiko, nori, and pickled ginger. Drizzle with a touch of sesame oil.

    10 min

  4. 4

    Serve immediately

    The bowl waits for no one. Serve with a small dish of soy sauce and wasabi on the side. Instruct guests not to mix — each element should be tasted separately and together.

    2 min

Chef's Kitchen Notes

1.

Sushi-grade fish must be purchased from a trusted fishmonger the morning you plan to serve.

2.

The rice temperature is as important as the fish quality. Too cold and it becomes starchy; too warm and the fish warms uncomfortably.

3.

Chirashi is a canvas. The arrangement communicates care — take two minutes to compose it beautifully.

Recipe by

Chef Marcus Thorne

Head of Culinary Innovation

Marcus spent a decade in Tokyo mastering Japanese knife craft before returning to London to lead the kitchens at two Bib Gourmand restaurants. He believes precision is poetry — every cut, every temperature, every timing tells the story of the dish. His Japanese and East Asian menus are renowned for their meditative attention to technique.

Chef's Tip

Pro Kitchen Insight

“The quality of chirashi is entirely dependent on the quality of your fish. There is nowhere to hide. Source the very best you can afford and let the fish speak.”

— Chef Marcus Thorne

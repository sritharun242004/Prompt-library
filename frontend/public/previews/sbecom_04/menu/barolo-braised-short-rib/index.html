Barolo-Braised Short Rib

4h total
Intermediate
Serves 2
4.8
Chef Elena Rossi

A slow-braised short rib in a full bottle of Barolo with root vegetables and gremolata — Sunday cooking at its most luxurious. Three hours in the oven rewards you with meat that falls apart at the suggestion of a fork.

Prep

30 min

Cook

3h 30min

Total

4h

Mise en Place

2 lbs Bone-in short ribs

trimmed of excess fat

750 ml Barolo or robust red wine

500 ml Beef stock

2 medium Carrots

roughly chopped

2 stalks Celery stalks

roughly chopped

1 large Yellow onion

quartered

6 cloves Garlic

smashed

2 tbsp Tomato paste

4 sprigs Fresh thyme

2 leaves Bay leaves

1/4 cup Flat-leaf parsley

finely chopped, for gremolata

1 lemon Lemon zest

finely grated, for gremolata

Method

  1. 1

    Sear the ribs

    Pat ribs completely dry. Season aggressively with salt and pepper. In a Dutch oven over high heat, sear all four sides in clarified butter until deep mahogany — 3 minutes per side. Do not crowd the pan.

    Chef's Note

    This colour is flavour. Every minute of patience here pays dividends in the braise.

    15 min

  2. 2

    Build the soffritto

    In the same pan, reduce heat to medium. Add carrot, celery, and onion. Cook until softened and lightly golden, about 8 minutes. Add garlic and tomato paste; stir until the paste darkens, about 2 minutes.

    10 min

  3. 3

    Deglaze and braise

    Pour in the wine and bring to a boil, scraping up all fond. Add stock, thyme, and bay leaves. Nestle the ribs back in — they should be nearly submerged. Bring to a simmer, cover, and transfer to 325°F (165°C) oven.

    10 min

  4. 4

    Slow braise

    Braise for 3 to 3.5 hours until the meat is falling-tender. Remove ribs carefully; strain and reduce the braising liquid to a glossy sauce.

    3h 30min

  5. 5

    Finish with gremolata

    Combine parsley and lemon zest. Plate over creamy polenta, spoon sauce generously, and finish with gremolata.

    5 min

Chef's Kitchen Notes

1.

Make this a day ahead — braised dishes are always better on day two.

2.

The wine quality matters. Cook with something you would drink.

3.

Reduce the braising liquid aggressively — the sauce should coat a spoon thickly.

Step-by-Step Guide

Restaurant-grade crust using the Maillard reaction — controlled heat and patience are the only tools you need.

  1. 1

    Pat protein completely dry with paper towels — moisture is the enemy of caramelisation.

  2. 2

    Season generously with salt at least 30 minutes before cooking; allow the brine to be reabsorbed.

  3. 3

    Heat a heavy pan (cast iron or stainless) until a drop of water evaporates instantly.

  4. 4

    Add a high-smoke-point fat (clarified butter or grapeseed oil) and wait for first wisps of smoke.

  5. 5

    Place protein away from you into the pan; do not move it for 2–3 minutes.

  6. 6

    Flip once when a crust releases cleanly. Baste with butter, thyme, and garlic in the final minute.

Osso Buco alla Milanese

2h 30min total
Intermediate
Serves 4
4.9
Chef Elena Rossi

Cross-cut veal shanks braised in white wine and gremolata until the marrow trembles in the bone — Milan's most celebrated dish, served with saffron risotto Milanese in the classic pairing that has never been improved upon.

Prep

30 min

Cook

2h

Total

2h 30min

Mise en Place

4 pieces Veal shanks

4cm thick, tied with kitchen string

250 ml Dry white wine

400 ml Veal stock

2 medium Carrots

finely diced

2 stalks Celery

finely diced

1 large White onion

finely diced

200 g Canned plum tomatoes

crushed by hand

3 cloves Garlic

minced

1/3 cup Flat-leaf parsley

finely chopped — for gremolata

1 lemon Lemon zest

finely grated — for gremolata

1 clove Garlic

minced — for gremolata

3 tbsp Plain flour

for dredging

Method

  1. 1

    Prepare and sear the shanks

    Dredge shanks in flour, shaking off excess. Season generously. Sear in clarified butter until deeply browned on all sides. The bone should caramelise. Remove.

    Chef's Note

    Tie each shank with string around its circumference to prevent the meat from falling off the bone during braising.

    20 min

  2. 2

    Build the soffritto

    Reduce heat. Add diced carrot, celery, and onion to the same pan. Cook until softened and golden, 10 minutes. Add garlic, cook 1 minute.

    12 min

  3. 3

    Deglaze and braise

    Pour in wine and reduce by half. Add crushed tomatoes and stock. Nestle shanks back in, cover, and braise at 160°C (325°F) for 1.5–2 hours until meat is falling from the bone.

    2h

  4. 4

    Make the gremolata

    Combine parsley, lemon zest, and minced garlic. This is added at the very end — gremolata is brightness, never cooked.

    5 min

  5. 5

    Serve

    Remove shanks carefully, reduce sauce if needed. Plate over saffron risotto Milanese. Spoon sauce generously over the shank and scatter gremolata. Scoop marrow from the bone at the table.

    5 min

Chef's Kitchen Notes

1.

The marrow is not a garnish — it is the dish. Use a cocktail spoon to scoop it and spread on bread or mix into the risotto.

2.

Gremolata must be added off the heat. Heat destroys its volatile citrus aromatics.

3.

Make osso buco a day ahead. The braising liquid concentrates further overnight in the refrigerator.

Step-by-Step Guide

The art of collagen conversion — how patient heat transforms tough cuts into fork-tender magnificence.

  1. 1

    Choose the right cut — shoulder, short rib, shank, cheeks. Lean cuts dry out when braised.

  2. 2

    Sear in batches over high heat for maximum Maillard crust. Never crowd the pan.

  3. 3

    Build aromatics: mirepoix, tomato paste, herbs. Cook until the paste darkens to rust.

  4. 4

    Deglaze with wine and reduce by half to cook off alcohol before adding stock.

  5. 5

    Liquid should come three-quarters up the protein, never completely submerge.

  6. 6

    Cover tightly and braise at 160–170°C (325°F). Check every 45 minutes.

  7. 7

    The protein is done when it yields with almost no resistance to a cake tester.

  8. 8

    Strain braising liquid, reduce to a glossy sauce, and mount with cold butter.

Chef's Tip

Pro Kitchen Insight

“The gremolata is what separates osso buco from any other braise. Its role is purely to provide a citrus-herb brightness that cuts through three hours of slow-cooked richness. Never skip it and never add it too early.”

— Chef Elena Rossi

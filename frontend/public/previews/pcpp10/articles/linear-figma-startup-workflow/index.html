The Quiet Consolidation of the Startup Workflow Stack

Linear, Figma, and Notion have each crossed $500 million in annual recurring revenue, and together they have replaced a generation of enterprise workflow tools at startups. The consolidation raises questions about what happens when these platforms raise prices.

Casey Newton·April 8, 2025·5 min read
ShareShareCopy link

The startup tool stack has stabilized in a way that would have seemed surprising five years ago. Linear for engineering project management, Figma for design, Notion for documentation and knowledge management — these three products have achieved dominant positions at companies of between 10 and 500 employees with a speed that reflects genuine product quality rather than salesforce aggression. All three have crossed $500 million in ARR, and all three are now large enough that their pricing decisions have material impact on their customers' operating costs.

The platform dependency question is becoming more urgent as these tools mature and their pricing reflects their market position rather than their growth-phase acquisition economics. Linear raised prices by 40% in late 2024 and reported minimal churn — a data point that reveals something important about the switching costs involved in migrating engineering project history and workflow to a competing product. Figma's proposed acquisition by Adobe, which failed on antitrust grounds, revealed that the company's price-to-revenue multiple implied pricing power that product metrics now support.

The paradox of the startup workflow stack is that it was built by and for companies that value flexibility and avoid vendor lock-in, but has produced a set of dependencies that are structurally similar to the enterprise software lock-in it was designed to replace. Switching costs for a 50-person startup that has three years of Linear tickets and Figma files are not trivially different from the switching costs for an enterprise migrating off SAP. This dynamic is not a criticism of the products, which are genuinely excellent; it is an observation about the structural economics of workflow software that applies regardless of quality.

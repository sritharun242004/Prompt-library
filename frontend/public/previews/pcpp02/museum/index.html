The Weird Lens Museum

8 lenses — oddities, soviet glass, and optical anomalies worth knowing.

Helios 44-2 58mm f/2

35mm / Full Frame · M42 (adapter to Nikon/Sony) · 1975

The king of swirly bokeh. The 8-blade aperture and spherical aberration create a characteristic vortex blur. Best wide open at f/2 on subjects 1–2m away.

Jupiter-9 85mm f/2

35mm / Full Frame · M42 (adapter) · 1961

Soviet copy of the Carl Zeiss Sonnar 85mm. Produces beautiful, smooth out-of-focus rendering. The 15-blade aperture creates near-circular bokeh even stopped down.

Meyer-Optik Trioplan 100mm f/2.8

35mm / Full Frame · M42 (adapter) · 1939

The original "soap bubble bokeh" lens. The 3-element Trioplan renders out-of-focus lights as hollow rings. Infamous and nearly impossible to replicate digitally.

Petzval 58mm f/1.9

35mm / Full Frame · Nikon F / Canon EF · 1862

Revived Petzval design from Lomography. The 1862 optical formula creates strong field curvature — only the centre is sharp, with the periphery swirling dramatically.

Spiratone Portragon 100mm f/4

35mm / Full Frame · T-mount (adapter) · 1970

A single-element lens — literally one piece of glass. Produces a uniquely soft, painterly image with no chromatic correction. Every portrait looks like a watercolour.

Nikon 28-50mm f/3.5 AI-S

35mm / Full Frame · Nikon F · 1982

The overlooked corner of the Nikon vintage lineup. Tack sharp from 28–50mm, superb sunstars with 7 straight blades, and tiny enough for a jacket pocket. No flaws.

Zenitar 16mm f/2.8 Fisheye

35mm / Full Frame · M42 (adapter) · 1991

Soviet full-frame fisheye. Produces heavy barrel distortion (220° diagonal FOV) and strong colour aberration wide open. Both are features, not bugs.

Voigtländer Nokton 35mm f/1.4

35mm / Full Frame · Leica M · 2001

The go-to fast 35mm for Leica M bodies. Renders with classic "radioactive" glow at f/1.4 (thoriated glass element) that no modern lens can replicate.

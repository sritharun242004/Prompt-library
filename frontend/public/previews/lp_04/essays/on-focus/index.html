On Focus: The Scarcest Resource

2024-04-20

Focus is the scarcest resource in the information economy. Raw intelligence is fairly common. Hard work, properly directed, is available to most people. But the ability to focus deeply on a single important problem for a sustained period—that is rare, increasingly rare, and extraordinarily valuable. The entire modern media and technology industry is designed to extract attention. Understanding this is the beginning of reclaiming it.

The mechanics of focus are simple but not easy. You need long blocks of uninterrupted time—at minimum ninety minutes, ideally three to four hours. You need to eliminate the notifications, the interruptions, the context-switching that fragment attention into unusable shards. You need a workspace that signals to your nervous system that it is time to think, not to react. And you need practice—focus, like a muscle, atrophies without use and strengthens with disciplined exercise.

The deeper issue with focus is not mechanical but philosophical: you must have something worth focusing on. Most people lack clarity about what they are actually trying to accomplish. Without that clarity, focus has nowhere to land. The practice of ruthlessly narrowing your objectives—of deciding not just what to do but what not to do—is upstream of focus itself. The person who is clear about what matters to them and why does not struggle to focus. The struggle for focus is often a symptom of unclear purpose.

On Mortality as a Clarifying Force

2023-12-10

The Stoic practice of memento mori—remembering death—was not morbid. It was clarifying. When you genuinely internalize the fact that your time is finite and the end is coming, your priorities become unusually clear. The petty concerns, the social anxieties, the endless comparison to others—these dissolve when placed against the backdrop of mortality. What remains is a small number of things that actually matter.

Most people do not think about death because it is uncomfortable. This is understandable but costly. The discomfort of contemplating death is mild and temporary. The cost of not contemplating it is that you organize your life around the wrong things—around comfort, approval, convenience—rather than around meaning and genuine contribution. The examined relationship with mortality is one of the most productive philosophical practices available.

Death also clarifies the nature of time. Every day you live, you are spending a non-renewable resource. There is no saving time for later. The question is not whether you will spend your time, but on what. This framing makes the cost of distraction concrete. An hour spent on social media is an hour that will not return. A relationship left unrepaired is an opportunity that may close permanently. A project left unstarted may wait forever. Mortality is the most honest teacher available, and the lesson is always the same: now is the time.

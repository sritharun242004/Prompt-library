On Reading: Why Books Are the Best Technology

2024-08-01

The book is the greatest technology for transmitting knowledge ever invented. A book compresses years of a person's thinking into a format that can be consumed in days. The economics are extraordinary: for a few dollars and a few hours, you can access the complete mental model of someone who spent decades developing it. No other medium comes close to this density of signal per unit of time.

The problem is that most people read wrong. They read for information—to be able to say they have read the book, to have a fact to cite in conversation. This is almost useless. The correct approach is to read for understanding. Read slowly enough to actually think about what you are reading. Stop when something surprises you and ask why it surprises you—your surprise is revealing the shape of your existing model. Close the book and try to explain what you have learned in your own words. Only then do you actually know it.

The choice of what to read matters as much as how you read. Most books published each year are noise. The best books are old books—the ones that have survived the filter of time. If a book is still being read fifty years after publication, there is a reason. Read the classics in every field you care about. Read Feynman on physics, Darwin on evolution, Montaigne on the human condition, Adam Smith on economics. These books contain more value per page than almost any contemporary work. They also have the advantage of making all subsequent reading easier—they are the foundation on which everything else is built.

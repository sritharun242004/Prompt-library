On Making Decisions Under Uncertainty

2024-06-15

Most decisions are made with incomplete information. This is not a problem to be solved—it is the permanent condition of being human. The question is how to make good decisions despite uncertainty, and how to build a life where your decisions compound positively over time even when individual choices are imperfect.

The most useful frame for decisions under uncertainty is expected value combined with asymmetric payoffs. An expected-value calculation asks: if I made this decision a thousand times, what would the average outcome be? But the calculation is only half the work. You also need to examine the shape of the outcome distribution. A bet with positive expected value but catastrophic downside risk may not be worth taking if the downside would take you out of the game. Conversely, a bet with modest expected value but enormous upside and limited downside is often worth taking even if the probability of success is low.

The second principle is to distinguish between reversible and irreversible decisions. Most decisions are reversible—you can update, correct, and change course. These decisions should be made quickly, with whatever information you have, because the cost of delay often exceeds the cost of a wrong decision you can fix. Irreversible decisions deserve much more care. You cannot unfire someone you have fired, cannot unbreak certain relationships, cannot undo certain reputational decisions. The asymmetry is important: be fast on reversible decisions, deliberate on irreversible ones.

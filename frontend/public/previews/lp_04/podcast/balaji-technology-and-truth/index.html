Show notes

Naval and Balaji Srinivasan discuss the intersection of technology, media, and governance. Topics include the decline of legacy institutions, the rise of the network state, and how technology changes the balance of power between individuals and institutions.

00:06:30Naval

You are one of the most prolific technology optimists I know. Make the case for why the next twenty years are better than the last twenty.

00:06:44Balaji

Computation continues to get cheaper. Biology is being digitized—we can now read and write DNA the way we read and write software. AI is accelerating scientific discovery. The constraints that have limited human life for all of history—disease, poverty, geographic isolation—are all being addressed simultaneously.

00:30:15Naval

Explain the network state concept for someone who has not read the book.

00:30:28Balaji

A network state is a highly aligned online community with a collective goal, a shared consciousness, and the capacity to crowdfund territory. It starts as a social network, becomes an economy, and eventually gains diplomatic recognition. It is the startup version of a country.

00:55:00Naval

Is the nation-state obsolete?

00:55:12Balaji

Not yet—but it is losing its monopoly on legitimacy. When people can transact globally without banks, communicate globally without media companies, and eventually live in communities organized by values rather than geography, the monopoly of the nation-state erodes.

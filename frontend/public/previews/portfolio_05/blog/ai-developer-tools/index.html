AI coding tools are changing what junior engineers learn first

May 18, 20244 min read
aitoolscareer

When I was a junior engineer, the first thing I got good at was reading stack traces. Not because anyone taught me — because I was constantly breaking things and had to figure out why. The stack trace was the primary feedback loop. Over months, I developed a mental model of how the runtime worked by reading thousands of error messages.

That feedback loop is changing. With AI-assisted coding, the space between "write code" and "working code" has compressed. You still get stack traces when things break, but you get them less often, and when you do, there's a shortcut to explanation (paste into the chat). The debugging instinct — that patient, methodical unpacking of what the runtime is actually doing — is getting less exercise.

I'm not sure this is bad. A lot of what junior engineers spent time on wasn't learning; it was tedium. Spending four hours finding a missing semicolon teaches you to use a linter, not to think better. If AI tools eliminate the tedious part of the debugging loop, what's left might be the more valuable part: understanding why the system behaves the way it does.

What the tools are actually good at

After a year of using AI coding assistants seriously, the pattern I've noticed is that they're excellent at the boring-but-necessary and poor at the novel-and-ambiguous.

Boilerplate: excellent. Typing out the structure of a TypeScript interface, a CRUD route, a React form with validation — the AI handles it faster and more correctly than most people. Not because it's doing something smart, but because these are patterns that appear identically thousands of times in its training data.

Refactoring: good when the goal is clear, poor when the codebase has idiosyncrasies. Ask it to extract a function and it will. Ask it to restructure an auth module that has accrued four years of workarounds and it will produce something that looks right but doesn't account for the edge cases the workarounds were handling.

Novel problems: weak. When you're working at the edge of what's documented — a new API, an unusual performance constraint, an interaction between two systems that wasn't designed to interact — the AI's suggestions are often plausible-sounding and wrong. This is the dangerous category, because the confidence of the output doesn't track its correctness.

The judgment gap

The thing AI tools can't do is exercise judgment about the system you're actually building. They can generate code that satisfies a prompt. They can't tell you whether the prompt describes what you actually need.

This is the skill that becomes more valuable as AI tools get better, not less. The engineer who can look at an AI-generated implementation and say "this is technically correct but it will be a maintenance problem in six months because of how this module is organized" is more useful than ever, because that judgment is increasingly the bottleneck.

The engineers I've seen get the most out of AI tools are the ones who use them for generation and bring their own judgment to the review. They treat the AI as a fast typist with broad knowledge and no taste. The output is raw material. The engineering is in deciding what to keep.

What this means for how you learn

If you're early in your career, the risk is that AI tools let you stay in a comfort zone where things mostly work without you fully understanding why. The habit to resist is accepting the first output that runs. The habit to build is the question: "What would I need to understand to have written this myself?"

That question forces the learning that the feedback loop used to force. It's slower in the moment. It compounds faster over time.

My Failure Resume

8 times I failed.
Publicly documented.

Most people only share their wins. I think that's dishonest. Here are all my major failures — what really happened, not the sanitised LinkedIn version. If one of these saves you from repeating my mistake, it was worth writing.

VCs who rejected me

2005

Dropped out of Engineering

What went wrong

I joined an engineering college because everyone around me was doing it. After two years I realised I had zero interest in circuits and zero plan for my life. I dropped out with no backup.

What I learned

Peer pressure is the most expensive decision-making framework. Do the uncomfortable work of figuring out what YOU want before committing two years and your parents' money.

2007

First Business — Printed T-Shirts

What went wrong

I invested ₹80,000 of borrowed money into a custom T-shirt printing business. I had no customers, no distribution, and no understanding of unit economics. The stock sat in boxes for a year.

What I learned

Validate demand before you spend. One pre-order is worth more than a thousand encouraging nods from friends.

2009

EdTech Startup — TutorPoint

What went wrong

Built a tutoring marketplace for 14 months. Got 200 sign-ups but never charged a single rupee. I was terrified of pricing. We ran out of runway and shut down.

What I learned

Not charging is not generosity — it is fear. Pricing is a skill. Learn it early or die free.

2011

Angel Investment in a Food Delivery App

What went wrong

I put ₹6 lakh into a friend's food delivery startup before Swiggy existed. The founder pivoted three times in six months, burned through capital on ads, and shut down. I lost everything.

What I learned

Never invest in people you cannot say no to. Friendship and investing are different skill sets — keep them separate.

2013

SaaS Tool for HR Teams — HireFlow

What went wrong

We built a beautiful product for 18 months based on feedback from HR managers who never had the budget authority to buy. We confused interest with intent.

What I learned

Talk to the person with the credit card, not the person with the opinion. Willingness to pay and willingness to talk are completely different things.

2015

YouTube Channel — First Attempt

What went wrong

I started a YouTube channel about entrepreneurship. Posted 22 videos in four months. Average views: 47. I quit, convinced I had nothing worth saying.

What I learned

Distribution is a skill you develop, not a gift you get. I was comparing my Day 1 to someone else's Day 1000. I came back in 2019 and did not quit.

2017

Fitness App — FitBharat

What went wrong

Co-founded a fitness app targeting Tier-2 Indian cities. We raised ₹1.4 Cr from friends and family. The co-founder disagreement over product direction became so toxic that we shut down rather than fight it out. Both of us walked away with nothing.

What I learned

A co-founder relationship is harder than a marriage. Have the difficult conversation about values, equity, and decision-making authority BEFORE you take money, not after.

2019

Misread the NFT Wave

What went wrong

I publicly called NFTs "the future of creator monetisation" in a LinkedIn post that reached 400K people. Six months later the market collapsed. I had staked my credibility on trend-chasing.

What I learned

Your audience trusts you more than you deserve on topics you haven't deeply studied. Say "I don't know" more. It is not weakness — it is the most defensible position.

A note on radical transparency

I share these failures because I spent years consuming content that only showed the glamorous side of entrepreneurship. I don't know if sharing my failures makes me brave or foolish. But I do know it makes me honest — and that's more valuable to me than appearing successful.
